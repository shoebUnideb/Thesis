5.1 ML Performance on Clean Data
──────────────────────────────────

The starting point for all comparisons is the performance of the three machine
learning models on the clean, noise-free dataset. This establishes what is
achievable under ideal conditions and provides the reference against which all
subsequent noise and parameter-sweep results are interpreted. Table 5.1 summarises
the mean RMSE, MAE, and R² across five random seeds at the full 501-wavelength
configuration.

      TABLE 5.1. Clean-data ML performance at 501 wavelengths (mean ± std, 5 seeds)
      ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
      Model              RMSE (nm)          MAE (nm)           R²
      ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
      Linear Regression  161.21 ± 47.65     105.25 ± 35.81    −102.20 ± 74.37
      Random Forest        1.86 ± 0.26        1.17 ± 0.11       0.9875 ± 0.0038
      MLP                  1.56 ± 0.38        1.04 ± 0.18       0.9912 ± 0.0037
      ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The Linear Regression result is, as anticipated, catastrophically poor. An RMSE of
161 nm on a thickness range of only 60 nm (20 to 80 nm) means the model is predicting
values far outside the physical range of the training data. The R² of −102 is not
a typo: a negative R² means the model performs worse than simply predicting the mean
thickness for every sample, which is the baseline a constant predictor achieves.
This is a direct consequence of the underdetermined problem setup, with 1,002 features
and roughly 244 training samples, and is entirely expected. The large standard
deviation across seeds (±47.65 nm) reflects the fact that small changes in the
particular training subset cause large swings in the weight vector, which is the
hallmark of an unstable, unconstrained least-squares solution.

The Random Forest achieves a mean RMSE of 1.86 ± 0.26 nm and an R² of 0.9875. The
MLP does slightly better: 1.56 ± 0.38 nm and R² = 0.9912. These are strong results.
To put the numbers in context, the training target range spans 60 nm and the achieved
RMSE is under 2 nm, a relative error of around 3%. For the MLP, the prediction
error is on average just over one nanometre in absolute terms (MAE = 1.04 nm). The
standard deviation across seeds is small relative to the mean for both models (0.26
and 0.38 nm respectively), confirming that these numbers are stable and not the
result of a particularly lucky random split.

The modest advantage of MLP over RF (0.30 nm RMSE difference) is consistent with
the expectation that a neural network captures more of the nonlinear structure of
the mapping from spectrum to thickness. However, the difference is not large enough
to draw strong conclusions from the clean-data results alone. The more informative
comparison occurs under noise conditions, discussed in Section 5.2.

5.2 Noise Robustness Study
────────────────────────────

Table 5.2 shows the RMSE at 501 wavelengths for all six conditions (clean plus five
noise types) and all three models. The analytical solver results are included for
direct comparison. They are discussed in detail in Section 5.3.

      TABLE 5.2. RMSE (nm) at 501 wavelengths across all noise conditions
      ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
      Condition    Analytical    Linear Reg.        Random Forest      MLP
      ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
      Clean          0.000      161.21 ± 47.65     1.86 ± 0.26     1.56 ± 0.38
      Gaussian       0.130       21.82 ± 2.71      2.02 ± 0.34     1.57 ± 0.28
      Relative       4.293       17.97 ± 2.94      2.21 ± 0.21     1.66 ± 0.32
      Uniform        6.514       21.42 ± 5.55      2.06 ± 0.33     1.64 ± 0.23
      Bias           7.749      182.04 ± 46.76     1.97 ± 0.28     1.62 ± 0.53
      Mixed          4.048       30.15 ± 7.10      2.12 ± 0.19     1.32 ± 0.26
      ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Several patterns in this table are worth examining carefully.

Random Noise Degrades ML Gradually. Moving from clean to Gaussian noise, the RF
RMSE rises from 1.86 to 2.02 nm, an increase of 0.16 nm or about 9%. The MLP
rises from 1.56 to 1.57 nm, an increase so small it is essentially within the seed-
to-seed variation. Uniform noise, which is wider than Gaussian (effective standard
deviation about 0.58° versus 0.5° for Gaussian), produces similarly modest
degradation: RF reaches 2.06 nm, MLP reaches 1.64 nm. These results confirm that
both ensemble and neural network models absorb random noise well when they have
been trained on clean data, because the model implicitly averages over the noise
distribution present in the test spectra. The inversion problem remains well-
conditioned under symmetric noise.

Relative Noise is Moderately Challenging. Relative noise at the 1% level pushes
the RF to 2.21 nm and the MLP to 1.66 nm. These are slightly higher than the
random noise cases, which makes physical sense: relative noise perturbs the spectral
bands with the largest Ψ and Δ values most heavily, and those bands tend to carry
the most information about film thickness. A perturbation concentrated in the most
informative channels is therefore more damaging than a uniformly distributed one.

Systematic Bias Barely Affects RF and MLP. The bias condition (a constant +0.5°
offset on every Ψ and Δ value) results in RF RMSE = 1.97 ± 0.28 nm and MLP RMSE
= 1.62 ± 0.53 nm. These numbers are barely above the clean-data values. This is a
somewhat counterintuitive result: one might expect a constant offset to shift all
predictions systematically. The explanation is that the model learns a mapping from
spectrum shape to thickness, and a constant bias shifts the entire spectrum rigidly
while preserving its shape. The model can partly compensate for this because it has
seen spectra across five materials and 61 thicknesses during training, providing a
rich context that limits how far a rigid spectral shift can push the prediction
away from the correct answer. The wider standard deviation for the MLP under bias
(±0.53 nm versus ±0.26 nm for RF) suggests the neural network is somewhat more
sensitive to this effect when initialised differently, but the mean remains low.

Mixed Noise Produces the Best MLP Result. Most strikingly, the MLP achieves its
lowest RMSE of 1.32 ± 0.26 nm under mixed noise, actually better than on clean
data. This is not a numerical artefact. Mixed noise combines Gaussian random
variation with a constant bias, which adds variety to the training distribution and
acts as a form of implicit regularisation. The diversified noise exposure apparently
helps the MLP generalise better during training, because the early-stopping
criterion allows it to converge to a flatter minimum in weight space. The Random
Forest shows no such effect (2.12 nm under mixed, versus 1.86 nm clean), which is
consistent with RF being an ensemble of discrete trees rather than a smooth
continuous function. Trees do not benefit from noise-as-regularisation in the same
way a gradient-trained neural network does.

bars side by side within each group. Annotate the clean-data bars for reference.

5.3 Analytical Baseline Comparison (ML vs Classical Solver)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The analytical grid-search solver provides a fundamentally different kind of
reference. On clean data it achieves RMSE = 0.000 nm, exact to machine precision.
This result must be interpreted carefully. It arises because the test spectra were
generated by the same Fresnel forward model that the solver uses for inversion, with
no model mismatch and no noise. Under these conditions the solver is guaranteed to
find the exact answer: it is, in effect, solving its own equation. The result
therefore represents an idealised upper bound on analytical performance under
perfect model fidelity, not a claim about what would be achieved on real
experimental data. Real ellipsometry data contains instrument drift, surface
roughness, and approximation errors in the optical constants, all of which would
shift this number upward. With that caveat clearly stated, the clean-data result
still serves a valid purpose: it establishes the noise-free ceiling against which
the ML models and the degraded-noise analytical results can both be measured.

Under noise, the picture reverses sharply. Gaussian noise pushes the analytical
RMSE to just 0.130 nm, which still beats both ML models. This is because Gaussian
noise is zero-mean: on average, the minimum of the chi-squared cost function is
still at the correct thickness, and with 501 wavelength points the noise largely
averages out across the spectrum. The solver remains effective.

For relative noise, the analytical RMSE jumps to 4.293 nm, which is roughly twice
the MLP result (1.66 nm) and nearly double the RF result (2.21 nm). For uniform
noise it reaches 6.514 nm versus MLP 1.64 nm and RF 2.06 nm. For systematic bias,
the analytical RMSE of 7.749 nm is the worst of all, nearly five times the MLP
result (1.62 nm) and four times the RF result (1.97 nm). The failure mode of the
analytical solver under systematic noise is clear: a constant offset adds a rigid
shift to every data point, which moves the global minimum of chi-squared away from
the true thickness by a fixed, potentially large amount. Unlike a trained model, the
solver has no mechanism to absorb this shift. It trusts the measured spectrum as
ground truth.

      TABLE 5.3. Direct comparison: ML vs Analytical under each noise type
      ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
      Condition    Analytical    RF RMSE     MLP RMSE    ML Advantage (MLP)
      ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
      Clean         0.000 nm    1.86 nm      1.56 nm    Analytical wins
      Gaussian      0.130 nm    2.02 nm      1.57 nm    Analytical wins
      Relative      4.293 nm    2.21 nm      1.66 nm    ML wins  (×2.6)
      Uniform       6.514 nm    2.06 nm      1.64 nm    ML wins  (×4.0)
      Bias          7.749 nm    1.97 nm      1.62 nm    ML wins  (×4.8)
      Mixed         4.048 nm    2.12 nm      1.32 nm    ML wins  (×3.1)
      ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The crossover point is between Gaussian and relative noise. Below that level of
systematic or non-Gaussian noise, the physics-based solver is superior. Above it,
machine learning dominates. This result is the central quantitative finding of the
thesis: the advantage of ML is not universal but is specifically tied to how well
the noise distribution is captured in the training data.

[FIGURE 5.3: Bar chart comparing analytical RMSE, RF RMSE, and MLP RMSE under all
six noise conditions on a single y-axis (nm). Use logarithmic scale on the y-axis
so the clean analytical result (0.000 nm, an idealised perfect-fidelity value)
is handled as a near-zero value (set floor at 0.01 nm for display). The crossover from analytical-wins to ML-wins
between Gaussian and Relative should be visually clear.]


5.4 Study 1. Effect of Wavelength Range
──────────────────────────────────────────

Study 1 progressively reduces the number of wavelength points used as input
features from 501 down to 50. Table 5.4 shows selected results at representative
wavelength counts.

      TABLE 5.4. Study 1: RMSE vs wavelength count (clean data, mean ± std)
      ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
      Wavelengths    RF RMSE (nm)      MLP RMSE (nm)
      ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
          501        1.86 ± 0.26       1.56 ± 0.38
          300        1.83 ± 0.21       1.95 ± 0.31
          200        1.74 ± 0.32       1.92 ± 0.60
          150        1.72 ± 0.32       1.67 ± 0.39
          100        1.79 ± 0.26       1.50 ± 0.26
           75        1.71 ± 0.27       1.89 ± 0.48
           50        1.57 ± 0.21       1.98 ± 0.27
      ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The most striking observation is that for the Random Forest, RMSE is almost flat
across the entire range from 501 down to 50 wavelengths. The RF achieves 1.86 nm
at 501 points and 1.57 nm at 50 points, meaning it actually performs marginally
better with fewer features. This is a regularisation effect: the RF's random feature
subsampling at each tree split is more effective when the total number of features
is smaller, reducing the chance that a noisy feature is selected. The implication is
that the RF model does not need the full 501-wavelength spectrum to achieve its peak
accuracy. A 10-fold reduction in spectral density is essentially free.

The MLP shows more variation across the sweep. At 100 wavelengths it reaches its
best result of 1.50 nm, which is slightly better than 1.56 nm at the full 501.
At very low wavelength counts (50 points), it degrades to 1.98 nm, overtaking the
RF. The variation is larger across seeds at low wavelength counts, as evidenced by
the wider standard deviations, indicating that the particular evenly-spaced channels
selected matter more for the MLP than for the RF.

Both models remain well below 3 nm RMSE at all wavelength counts tested. This is
one of the most practically important findings in the study. An instrument that
measures at 50 evenly-spaced wavelengths across 300–800 nm can deliver comparable
accuracy to one measuring at 501, at a fraction of the spectral cost.

and MLP. Add ±1σ shaded bands around each line. Mark the 501-point baseline with
a vertical dashed line and the 100-point and 50-point positions with annotations.
The near-flat RF curve and the slight MLP dip around 100 wl are the key visual

5.5 Study 2. Effect of Training Dataset Size
───────────────────────────────────────────────

Study 2 reduces the number of distinct film thicknesses represented in the training
set from the full 61 values (every integer nm from 20 to 80) down to 1. Table 5.5
shows selected results.

      TABLE 5.5. Study 2: RMSE vs training dataset size (clean data, mean ± std)
      ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
      Thicknesses    Samples (total)    RF RMSE (nm)      MLP RMSE (nm)
      ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
           61            305            1.86 ± 0.26       1.56 ± 0.38
           50            250            2.23 ± 0.35       3.16 ± 1.45
           40            200            2.62 ± 0.53       2.54 ± 0.86
           30            150            3.30 ± 0.87       3.32 ± 1.01
           20            100            5.82 ± 1.85       9.96 ± 5.24
           10             50            9.31 ± 2.35      14.21 ± 6.47
            5             25           15.11 ± 4.72      34.37 ± 14.53
      ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The degradation with dataset size is, as expected, much steeper than the degradation
with wavelength reduction. Halving the training data from 61 to 30 thicknesses
pushes the RF to 3.30 nm and MLP to 3.32 nm, roughly double the full-data error.
Reducing to 20 thicknesses (100 samples total), the MLP error reaches approximately
10 nm, while the RF is more resilient at 5.82 nm. Below 20 thicknesses, both models
degrade sharply, and the standard deviation across seeds becomes very large,
indicating that individual split realisations dominate the outcome.

The contrast with the wavelength sweep is instructive. Cutting features by 10x
(from 501 to 50 wavelengths) costs essentially nothing. Cutting training data by
roughly the same factor (from 61 to 10 thicknesses) increases RF RMSE by 5x and
MLP RMSE by 9x. This confirms that the binding constraint on prediction accuracy
is the number of distinct training samples, not the number of spectral channels.
For practical deployment, this means it is far more important to include diverse
thickness values in the calibration set than to maximise spectral resolution.

The RF maintains better data efficiency than the MLP throughout the lower end of
the size range. At 10 thicknesses, the RF RMSE is 9.31 nm while the MLP is 14.21
nm. At 5 thicknesses, the disparity grows further. Random Forests are well-known
for their robustness to small sample counts because bagging and random feature
selection together provide strong implicit regularisation, whereas the MLP with
its fixed max_iter and early stopping cannot converge as reliably when the
validation split draws from an already-tiny dataset.

[FIGURE 5.5: Line plots of RMSE (nm) versus number of training thicknesses for RF
and MLP, with ±1σ shaded bands. Use a logarithmic x-axis. Mark the 30-thickness
and 10-thickness positions with vertical annotations. The substantially steeper MLP
degradation at low sample counts versus the more gradual RF decline is the key
feature to emphasise.]


5.6 Diagnostic Analysis
─────────────────────────

5.6.1 Residual Error Distributions

Figure 5.6 shows the distribution of signed prediction errors (predicted minus true
thickness) for each of the three models on the clean test set with seed 42. The
signed residual is more informative than the absolute error because it reveals
whether prediction errors are systematic (biased in one direction) or random
(symmetrically distributed around zero).

The Linear Regression residuals have a mean of approximately −29.50 nm, confirming
a strong systematic underprediction bias. This is consistent with the underdetermined
nature of the OLS solution: the weight vector is not unique, and for most random
splits it converges to a solution that systematically underestimates thickness. The
distribution is wide and multi-modal, spanning hundreds of nanometres.

The Random Forest residuals are centred very close to zero, with a mean of +0.27 nm.
The distribution is approximately symmetric and much narrower than LR, with most
errors falling within ±3 nm of zero. The near-zero mean confirms that RF makes no
systematic prediction error, its mistakes are random, not directional. This is
the hallmark of a well-generalising model.

The MLP residuals show a similarly zero-centred distribution, with a mean of
+0.21 nm. The width is comparable to RF with a slight tendency toward narrower
tails, consistent with the marginally lower RMSE. Like the RF, the MLP has no
meaningful systematic bias, what errors it makes are scattered evenly above and
below the true value.

5.6.2 Random Forest Feature Importance

The Random Forest Gini feature importance provides a direct, wavelength-resolved
measure of how much each spectral channel contributes to the ensemble's predictions.
The importance scores are normalised so they sum to one across all 1,002 features.

The cumulative importance analysis reveals that the Random Forest concentrates its
predictive power in a small number of wavelength channels. The top 24 wavelengths
by importance account for 50% of the total cumulative importance across the
ensemble, and the top 100 wavelengths account for approximately 80%. To reach 95%
of the total importance, only 205 wavelengths are needed. This means that roughly
300 of the 501 wavelength channels are providing relatively marginal additional
predictive value.

The high-importance channels are not uniformly distributed across the 300–800 nm
range. They cluster around spectral regions where the five materials show the
strongest and most distinct optical features: the UV edge of silicon absorption
around 350–420 nm, the GaAs critical point region near 400–450 nm, and the GaN
band edge transition near 365 nm. This makes physical sense. Spectral regions where
different materials have very different n and k values produce the most discriminative
Ψ and Δ shapes, and it is precisely those shapes that carry the most thickness
information when inverted.
