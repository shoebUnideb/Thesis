================================================================================
  ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This thesis investigates whether machine learning can outperform a physics-based
analytical solver for film thickness retrieval from spectroscopic ellipsometry data,
and how prediction accuracy depends on measurement noise, spectral resolution, and
training dataset size.

A synthetic dataset of 152,805 ellipsometric spectra (Ψ(λ) and Δ(λ)) was generated
using the Fresnel three-layer thin-film forward model, covering five semiconductor
materials, silicon, gallium arsenide, gallium nitride, germanium, and indium
phosphide, on a silicon dioxide substrate, at an angle of incidence of 70 degrees,
over wavelengths 300–800 nm and film thicknesses 20–80 nm. Five noise variants
were produced: Gaussian, relative, uniform, systematic bias, and mixed noise.

Three machine learning models were evaluated: Linear Regression, Random Forest
(100 trees), and a Multilayer Perceptron (256–128–64 neurons, ReLU activations,
early stopping). Results were averaged across five random seeds. On the clean
dataset, the MLP achieves RMSE = 1.56 ± 0.38 nm and R² = 0.9912. The Random Forest
achieves RMSE = 1.86 ± 0.26 nm. A physics-based grid-search solver was also
implemented as a baseline, achieving RMSE = 0.000 nm on clean data. This represents
an idealised upper bound under perfect model fidelity: the test spectra are generated
by the same Fresnel forward model used for inversion, so the result is not
generalisable to real experimental data where model mismatch is unavoidable.

Under noise, the analytical solver degrades sharply for non-Gaussian conditions,
reaching RMSE = 7.749 nm under systematic bias. Machine learning models remain
stable across all noise types, with the MLP achieving its best result of 1.32 nm
under mixed noise, better than on clean data. The ML advantage over the analytical
solver ranges from 2.6 to 4.8 times depending on noise type.

A wavelength reduction study (501 down to 50 points, 91 cases) shows that both
models maintain near-full accuracy with 100 wavelengths. A training dataset size
study (61 down to 1 thickness value, 61 cases) identifies dataset size, not spectral
resolution, as the binding constraint on accuracy. Random Forest feature importance
analysis reveals that 50% of predictive information is concentrated in 24 of the
501 wavelength channels.

The results establish a quantitative boundary between the regimes where physics-based
inversion and machine learning are respectively superior, and provide actionable
guidelines for cost-reduced ellipsometer design and calibration set requirements.

Key words: spectroscopic ellipsometry, thin film, machine learning, Random Forest,
multilayer perceptron, inverse problem, noise robustness, semiconductor metrology.

1.1 Background and Motivation
──────────────────────────────

Measuring how thick a thin film is, a layer perhaps 20 or 30 nanometres across,
deposited on a semiconductor wafer, sounds almost absurdly precise as a problem.
And yet, getting it wrong by even a few nanometres can render a transistor non-
functional, compromise a photovoltaic cell's efficiency, or introduce variability
into a production line that nobody can trace back to its source. This is the
practical reality behind spectroscopic ellipsometry, and it is what this thesis
is ultimately about.

Spectroscopic ellipsometry (SE) is a non-destructive optical measurement
technique that probes the change in polarisation state of light upon reflection
from a sample surface. Two angles are recorded, Psi (Ψ) and Delta (Δ), as a
function of wavelength, and from these, one can, in principle, extract the optical
constants and thickness of thin films with sub-nanometre sensitivity [6]. It has
become the standard characterisation tool across semiconductor manufacturing,
photovoltaic research, and optical coating development [7]. The physics behind it
is well-established, owing to foundational work on Fresnel reflection coefficients
and thin-film interference models going back several decades [9].

The problem, though, is not in measuring Ψ and Δ. That part is relatively
well-solved. The difficulty lies in the inverse step, going from measured spectra
back to a meaningful physical quantity like film thickness. This inversion is, in
the strict mathematical sense, ill-posed: the same measured spectrum can sometimes
be consistent with more than one physical solution, and real measurement noise
pushes the problem further from having a clean answer [23]. Classical analytical
approaches rely on model-based fitting, where one assumes a parametric form for the
optical constants and minimises the residual between measured and simulated spectra.
For a single well-characterised material, this works reasonably well. For multiple
materials, varying substrates, or noisy data, the approach quickly becomes fragile.

Machine learning offers a different angle on this. Rather than fitting a physical
model iteratively, one trains a model on a large set of (spectrum, thickness) pairs
and asks it to learn the mapping directly. Liu et al. [11] demonstrated this
approach cleanly in 2021, framing SE as a supervised regression problem and showing
that neural networks can recover optical constants and film thickness from Ψ/Δ
spectra with competitive accuracy. Since then, the field has moved quickly. Work
by Kwak et al. [17] extended this to real semiconductor stacks and noted explicitly
that learned models degrade more gracefully under measurement noise than their
analytical counterparts. Arunachalam et al. [12] showed that for atomic layer
deposition processes, a machine learning model retains strong accuracy even after
discarding 90% of the wavelength points, a surprising and practically important
result that directly motivated the wavelength range study carried out in this work.

What is notably absent from the existing literature, though, is a systematic,
multi-material comparison. Most published studies pick a single material. ZnO [12],
TiO2 [13], ITO [18], and report results for that one case. There is no work I am
aware of that simultaneously considers Si, GaAs, GaN, Ge, and InP, benchmarks
three different model classes against each other under identical conditions, and
then subjects all of that to a structured noise robustness study. That gap is
precisely what this thesis tries to address.


1.2 Problem Statement
──────────────────────

Let me state the problem plainly. Given a set of ellipsometric spectra (Ψ(λ) and
Δ(λ)) measured across a wavelength range of 300–800 nm from a semiconductor thin
film on an SiO₂ substrate, can a machine learning model predict the film thickness
more accurately, and more robustly under realistic measurement noise, than a
physics-based analytical inverse solver?

This is not a trivial question. The analytical solver has an advantage in the clean-
data scenario: if the forward model is exact and there is no noise, then a grid
search over the correct physical model will recover the true thickness almost
perfectly. ML, by contrast, is an approximator, it cannot do better than its
training data allows. So the interesting regime is the noisy one. Real instruments
have calibration drift, detector noise, thermal fluctuations, and systematic
offset errors. What happens to each approach when the measurement is imperfect?

There is also a practical question about data requirements. How many wavelength
points are actually needed to achieve good accuracy? And how many distinct thickness
samples must one include in the training set before a model starts to generalise
reliably? These questions have direct implications for measurement system design —
a faster instrument might trade spectral resolution for acquisition speed, and
knowing the acceptable trade-off is valuable.


1.3 Objectives
───────────────

The objectives of this work are, in order of logical dependency:

1. Construct a physically accurate synthetic dataset by simulating ellipsometric
   spectra (Ψ, Δ) for five semiconductor materials. Si, GaAs, GaN, Ge, and InP —
   on an SiO₂ substrate, over the wavelength range 300–800 nm and a thickness range
   of 20–80 nm, using the standard three-layer Fresnel thin-film model. Optical
   constants are taken from established experimental sources [1][2][3][4][5].

2. Model five realistic noise scenarios. Gaussian, relative (amplitude-dependent),
   uniform, systematic bias, and mixed noise, and generate noisy variants of the
   dataset to simulate real instrument behaviour.

3. Train and evaluate three machine learning models (Linear Regression, Random
   Forest, and Multilayer Perceptron) on the clean dataset, with statistical
   averaging over five random seeds to produce reliable mean ± standard deviation
   estimates of prediction error.

4. Conduct two systematic parameter studies on the ML models:
      Study 1 — Effect of wavelength range (501 down to 50 wavelength points, 91
                cases) on prediction accuracy.
      Study 2 — Effect of training dataset size (61 down to 1 distinct thickness
                values, 61 cases) on prediction accuracy.

5. Repeat the full ML study under each of the five noise conditions and compare
   the degradation patterns across noise types.

6. Implement a physics-based analytical baseline, a lookup-table grid search
   using the same Fresnel forward model, and compare its accuracy against the ML
   models under both clean and noisy conditions.

7. Perform diagnostic analyses: residual error distributions per model, per-
   material performance breakdown, and Random Forest feature importance as a
   function of wavelength.


1.4 Scope and Limitations
──────────────────────────

A few things this thesis does not do, and why.

It does not use real experimental data. All measurements are simulated using the
Fresnel forward model, which means the results are internally consistent but not
validated against a physical instrument. The decision to work with synthetic data
was deliberate, it allows exact ground truth, controlled noise injection, and
systematic sweep studies that would be practically impossible to run on a real
ellipsometer without enormous experimental effort. The trade-off is that the
quantitative RMSE values reported here apply to this simulation framework, they
should be treated as indicative rather than as absolute guarantees for real-world
performance.

It considers only single-layer thin films on a transparent substrate. Multi-layer
stacks, which are common in real device manufacturing, are not considered. The
three-layer model (ambient / film / substrate) is the simplest physically meaningful
configuration. Extending it to four or more layers would require a transfer matrix
approach and substantially more data [8].

The five materials chosen, Si, GaAs, GaN, Ge, InP, are representative common
semiconductors with well-characterised optical constants from the literature [1][2]
[3][4][24]. Other materials (e.g., oxides, metals, organic films) are out of scope.

The temperature control system (Section 3.3) is included in the system design
because thermal stability is a genuine experimental concern in ellipsometry: optical
constants are temperature-dependent, and measurement drift during acquisition
introduces systematic error [9]. The Peltier-based stabilisation system described
in Chapter 3 addresses this hardware dimension, even though it falls outside the
ML modelling pipeline.

Finally, the wavelength range studied is 300–800 nm. Below 300 nm, deep-UV
ellipsometry requires different instrumentation. Above 800 nm, near-infrared effects
become important for some materials. This visible-range focus is appropriate for
the semiconductor materials and thickness range considered here.


1.5 Structure of the Thesis
─────────────────────────────

Chapter 2 covers the theoretical background, ellipsometry principles, the Fresnel
model, noise sources, the inverse problem, and machine learning methods, along
with a review of related published work.

Chapter 3 describes the system design: the optical measurement setup, the
temperature control hardware, the choice of materials and models, and the overall
software architecture.

Chapter 4 details the implementation: how the dataset was generated, how noise was
modelled, how the ML pipeline was constructed, how the analytical solver was built,

Chapter 5 presents the results across all experiments, the clean-data ML study,
the noise robustness study, the analytical comparison, and the diagnostic analyses.

Chapter 6 discusses what the results mean, where the methods agree or diverge, what
the spectral importance plots tell us physically, and where the approach has honest
limitations.

Chapter 7 summarises the findings, states whether the objectives were met, and
outlines realistic directions for future work.

2.1 Spectroscopic Ellipsometry
────────────────────────────────

Spectroscopic ellipsometry is, at its core, a polarimetric measurement technique.
Light with a known polarisation state is directed at a sample surface at an oblique
angle of incidence, and the reflected beam is analysed to determine how that
polarisation has changed. The change encodes information about the optical
properties and thickness of whatever is sitting on the surface. What makes the
technique powerful is sensitivity: because it measures the ratio of two reflection
coefficients rather than absolute intensities, it is largely immune to source
fluctuations and detector drift, and it can resolve film thicknesses well below one
nanometre under good conditions [6].

The geometry is fixed in this work at an angle of incidence of 70 degrees, measured
from the surface normal. This is close to the Brewster angle for many semiconductors
and represents a common industrial configuration because the ellipsometric contrast
is highest near Brewster conditions [9]. Rotating-analyser or rotating-compensator
instruments record Ψ and Δ simultaneously across a wavelength range by dispersing
the reflected beam onto a photodiode array or CCD. The measurement is fast, non-
contact, and non-destructive, which makes it suitable for in-line process monitoring.

The two measured quantities are defined formally through the ratio of the complex
reflection coefficients for the two polarisation states. For light polarised parallel
to the plane of incidence (p-polarisation) and perpendicular to it (s-polarisation),
the reflection amplitudes r_p and r_s are complex numbers. Their ratio, denoted rho,
is written as:

      ρ = r_p / r_s = tan(Ψ) · exp(i·Δ)                              ...(2.1)

From this, the ellipsometric angles are extracted as:

      Ψ = arctan(|ρ|)           with  0° ≤ Ψ ≤ 90°                   ...(2.2)
      Δ = arg(ρ)                with  0° ≤ Δ ≤ 360°                  ...(2.3)

Ψ carries information about the amplitude ratio of the two polarisation components,
and Δ carries information about the phase difference introduced by the reflection.
Together, they form a two-dimensional spectrum over wavelength, Ψ(λ) and Δ(λ), that
is characteristic of the sample being measured.

[FIGURE 2.1: Schematic of a rotating-analyser spectroscopic ellipsometer showing
light source, polariser, sample, analyser, and detector. Indicate the angle of
incidence θ_0 = 70°, the incident and reflected beams, and the three-layer sample
stack (ambient / thin film / substrate). This is Figure 1 in most SE review papers.]

Interpreting those spectra requires a physical model of the sample. This is where
the theory of thin-film interference becomes necessary.


2.2 Optical Modelling of Thin Films
─────────────────────────────────────

The optical model used throughout this work is the standard three-layer configuration:
ambient (air, n = 1), semiconductor thin film of thickness d, and SiO₂ substrate.
Each layer is characterised by a complex refractive index N = n + ik, where n is
the real refractive index and k is the extinction coefficient, which describes
optical absorption. For a transparent material like SiO₂ at visible wavelengths,
k is essentially zero.

When light hits the interface between two materials, it partially reflects and
partially transmits. The amplitude of reflection depends on the refractive indices
and the polarisation state. For the interface between medium i (index N_i, angle
θ_i) and medium t (index N_t, angle θ_t), the Fresnel reflection coefficients are:

      r_s = (N_i·cos θ_i  −  N_t·cos θ_t) / (N_i·cos θ_i  +  N_t·cos θ_t)   ...(2.4)

      r_p = (N_t·cos θ_i  −  N_i·cos θ_t) / (N_t·cos θ_i  +  N_i·cos θ_t)   ...(2.5)

The refraction angle θ_t at each interface is given by Snell's law in its complex
form:

      N_i · sin θ_i  =  N_t · sin θ_t                                ...(2.6)

In a thin film, there is a second interface at the bottom of the film, and the
beams reflecting from the top and bottom interfaces interfere with each other. The
phase accumulated by a wave travelling once through the film is called the phase
thickness β:

      β = (2π / λ) · N_1 · d · cos θ_1                              ...(2.7)

where λ is the free-space wavelength, N_1 is the complex refractive index of the
film, d is the film thickness, and θ_1 is the refraction angle inside the film.
The total reflection amplitude for each polarisation, accounting for multiple
reflections at both interfaces, is given by the Airy formula:

      r_total = (r_01  +  r_12 · e^(−2iβ)) / (1  +  r_01 · r_12 · e^(−2iβ))  ...(2.8)

This formula applies separately to the s and p polarisations, using the
corresponding Fresnel coefficients from (2.4) and (2.5). Once r_total,p and
r_total,s are computed, the ellipsometric angles Ψ and Δ follow directly from
equations (2.1), (2.2), and (2.3).

[FIGURE 2.2: Diagram of the three-layer optical model. Show the ambient layer
(n_0 = 1), the thin film layer with complex index N_1 = n_1 + ik_1 and thickness d,

      n²(λ) = 1  +  A₁λ²/(λ²−C₁²)  +  A₂λ²/(λ²−C₂²)  +  A₃λ²/(λ²−C₃²)    ...(2.9)

where A₁, A₂, A₃ and C₁, C₂, C₃ are material-specific Sellmeier coefficients, and
λ is expressed in micrometres. SiO₂ has negligible absorption at visible wavelengths,
so k = 0 is assumed for the substrate.

152,805 data points in long format.


2.3 Noise and Uncertainty in Optical Measurements
────────────────────────────────────────────────────

No physical measurement is perfect. Spectroscopic ellipsometers are subject to
several categories of error, and understanding them matters both for designing the
noise model and for interpreting the results later in Chapter 5.

Detector shot noise arises from the quantum nature of light detection. Photons arrive
at the detector with statistical fluctuations that scale as the square root of signal
intensity. At low signal levels this dominates, at high signals it is usually
negligible. It is well-approximated by a Gaussian distribution, which motivates the
first noise model used here: zero-mean Gaussian noise added independently to each
Ψ and Δ measurement:

      Ψ_noisy(λ) = Ψ(λ)  +  ε_G,    ε_G ~ N(0, σ²),   σ = 0.5°    ...(2.10)
      Δ_noisy(λ) = Δ(λ)  +  ε_G

Relative noise arises when the measurement uncertainty scales with signal amplitude,
as occurs with multiplicative noise sources such as source intensity fluctuations.
This was modelled as:

      Ψ_noisy(λ) = Ψ(λ) · (1  +  ε_R),   ε_R ~ U(−α, +α),  α = 0.01  ...(2.11)
      Δ_noisy(λ) = Δ(λ) · (1  +  ε_R)

where ε_R is drawn independently at each wavelength and the 1% level represents
a typical instrument specification for high-quality commercial systems.

Uniform noise provides a worst-case flat-distribution counterpart to Gaussian noise,
with each perturbation equally likely anywhere within the range ±1°:

      Ψ_noisy(λ) = Ψ(λ)  +  ε_U,    ε_U ~ U(−1°, +1°)               ...(2.12)

Systematic bias represents a constant offset error, the kind introduced by
miscalibration of the analyser or polariser azimuth angle. It is the same at every
wavelength for a given measurement:

      Ψ_noisy(λ) = Ψ(λ)  +  b,    b = +0.5°  (constant)              ...(2.13)

Mixed noise combines Gaussian and bias simultaneously, producing a distribution that
is centred away from zero, a more realistic model of an instrument that has both
random fluctuations and a residual calibration offset.

[FIGURE 2.4: Illustrative comparison of the five noise types applied to a single
example Ψ(λ) spectrum (e.g., Si at d = 50 nm). Show the clean spectrum and five
noisy versions in one plot, or as five small subplots. This gives the reader a
visual intuition for the qualitative difference between noise types before the
formal results in Chapter 5.]

The motivations for choosing these five types are practical. Gaussian and uniform
represent symmetric random noise from different distributional families. Relative
noise captures the wavelength-dependent character of intensity-normalised instruments.
Bias represents systematic hardware miscalibration. Mixed noise is the most realistic
because real instruments rarely have only one source of error.


2.4 Inverse Problems in Ellipsometry
──────────────────────────────────────

The forward problem in ellipsometry is well-defined and computationally
straightforward: given material properties and film thickness, compute Ψ(λ) and
Δ(λ) via equations (2.4) through (2.8). The inverse problem, given measured Ψ(λ)
and Δ(λ), recover the film thickness, is considerably harder.

The standard analytical approach is nonlinear least-squares fitting. One minimises
a cost function of the form:

      χ² = Σ_λ [ (Ψ_meas(λ) − Ψ_model(λ,d))²  +  (Δ_meas(λ) − Δ_model(λ,d))² ]  ...(2.14)

over the free parameter d (assuming the optical constants are known from the
literature). This is an optimisation problem, and it can be solved iteratively
using gradient-based methods or, in the simple single-parameter case used here,
by a brute-force grid search over a sufficiently dense set of candidate thicknesses.
The grid search approach is exact in the limit of grid density and avoids local
minima entirely, which makes it the preferred analytical baseline for comparison.

In this work, the analytical solver evaluates equation (2.14) at every point on a
thickness grid from 1 to 150 nm in steps of 0.1 nm (1,491 candidate values) and
selects the minimum. With known optical constants, this fully specifies the problem.

The difficulty arises with noise. Adding noise to Ψ_meas and Δ_meas shifts the
global minimum of χ² away from the true thickness. For systematic noise, constant
bias, for example, the minimum shifts by a nearly fixed amount, which may be large
or small depending on the sensitivity of the spectra. For random noise, the minimum
fluctuates from measurement to measurement, introducing scatter in the predictions.
Crucially, the analytical solver has no robustness mechanism. It trusts the
measured spectra exactly, with no regularisation or generalisation from prior
training data. This is precisely where machine learning, trained on many (spectrum,
thickness) pairs, gains its advantage: it implicitly averages over the noise
distribution it saw during training [11][17].

This theoretical asymmetry, clean-data advantage to the analytical solver and noisy-
data advantage to ML, is the central hypothesis this thesis tests quantitatively.


2.5 Machine Learning for Regression
──────────────────────────────────────

Three machine learning models are evaluated in this work. The choice covers a
deliberate range of complexity, from the simplest possible linear model through to
a moderately deep neural network.

Linear Regression (LR) assumes a linear mapping between input features (the 1,002
columns of Ψ and Δ values at 501 wavelengths each) and the output (thickness in nm):

      d_pred = w^T · x  +  b                                          ...(2.15)

where w is the weight vector and b is a bias term. The ordinary least-squares
solution minimises the sum of squared errors over the training set. This model is
included primarily as a reference. With 1,002 input features and only 305 × 0.8 =
244 training samples, the system is heavily underdetermined, so LR is not expected
to perform well. It doesn't. Its inclusion in the results chapter is nonetheless
useful as a baseline that illustrates how much the nonlinear models gain over a
linear one.

Random Forest Regression (RF) is an ensemble of decision trees [21]. Each tree is
trained on a bootstrap sample of the training data and a random subset of features
at each split. Predictions from all 100 trees are averaged. This averaging process
reduces variance substantially compared to a single tree and produces a model that
generalises well even in high-dimensional input spaces [22]. A practically important
byproduct is the Gini feature importance score, which measures how much each input
feature (each wavelength channel) contributed to the variance reduction across all
trees. This provides direct, quantitative information about which spectral regions
matter most for thickness prediction, and that information is physically interpretable.
The RF in this work uses 100 trees. No feature pre-selection or dimensionality
reduction is applied before training.

The Multilayer Perceptron (MLP) is a fully connected feedforward neural network.
The architecture used has three hidden layers with 256, 128, and 64 neurons
respectively, with ReLU (Rectified Linear Unit) activations:

      z^(l) = W^(l) · a^(l−1)  +  b^(l)                              ...(2.16)
      a^(l) = max(0, z^(l))                                            ...(2.17)

A linear output layer gives the final prediction. Input features are standardised
to zero mean and unit variance before training. The model is trained with a maximum
of 2,000 iterations using the Limited-memory BFGS (L-BFGS) solver, with 10% of the
training data held back as a validation set and early stopping applied to prevent
overfitting. The MLP has the highest representational capacity of the three models
and was found in preliminary experiments to produce the best accuracy on clean data.

[FIGURE 2.5: Architecture diagram of the MLP. Show the input layer with 1,002
nodes (Ψ_300 ... Ψ_800, Δ_300 ... Δ_800), three hidden layers (256, 128, 64 neurons)
with ReLU activations, and a single output node for thickness in nm. This is a
standard two-paragraph figure, keep it clean.]

Training and evaluation throughout this work follows a stratified random split: 80%
of samples for training, 20% for testing, where stratification is by material class
to ensure each of the five materials appears in both splits proportionally. To obtain
reliable error estimates rather than lucky or unlucky single-seed results, every
experiment is repeated five times under different random seeds (42, 0, 1, 7, 13).
These seeds control both the data split and the model initialisation for RF and MLP.
Results are reported as the mean and standard deviation across the five seeds.

Three performance metrics are computed throughout:

      RMSE = sqrt( (1/n) · Σ_i (d_pred,i − d_true,i)² )              ...(2.18)

      MAE  = (1/n) · Σ_i |d_pred,i − d_true,i|                       ...(2.19)

      R²   = 1  −  Σ_i (d_pred,i − d_true,i)² / Σ_i (d_true,i − d̄)²  ...(2.20)

RMSE is reported in nanometres throughout. It penalises large errors more heavily
than MAE and is more sensitive to outliers, which is appropriate here because a
10 nm error is qualitatively worse than ten 1 nm errors. R² is reported for
completeness and to facilitate comparison with published results in the literature,
where it is commonly used.


2.6 Temperature Effects and Control Systems
─────────────────────────────────────────────

2.6.1 Temperature Influence on Optical Measurements

Temperature is a parameter that tends to be treated as fixed in ellipsometry
calculations, and in a laboratory setting kept at 20–22°C with good HVAC control,
this is a reasonable approximation. But it is not always a safe one. The refractive
indices of semiconductors are temperature-dependent through both thermal expansion
and the temperature dependence of the electronic band structure. For silicon, Green
(2008) [2] explicitly provides temperature coefficients for n and k, noting that
the refractive index at 633 nm changes by approximately 1.5 × 10⁻⁴ per Kelvin. Over
a 10 K drift, that amounts to roughly a 1.5 × 10⁻³ shift in n, small but not
negligible when fitting for thicknesses at the nanometre level.

The more immediate practical problem is thermal drift during a measurement sweep.
A spectroscopic ellipsometer acquires data over a finite time, typically seconds to
a few minutes depending on the detector and spectral resolution. If the sample
temperature drifts during this window, the early and late wavelength channels are
effectively measuring different optical systems. The resulting spectrum has an
internal inconsistency that no amount of model sophistication can fully correct for.

2.6.2 Peltier-Based Temperature Control

Peltier thermoelectric modules (thermoelectric coolers, TECs) offer a compact and
electronically controllable means of stabilising sample temperature. A TEC pumps heat
from its cold side to its hot side at a rate proportional to the current through it.
By placing the sample on the cold side and using a PID (Proportional-Integral-
Derivative) controller in a closed loop with a temperature sensor, one can maintain
the sample within a fraction of a Kelvin relative to setpoint.

[FIGURE 2.6: Block diagram of the Peltier temperature control system. Show the TEC
module, the temperature sensor (thermocouple or NTC thermistor) mounted near the
sample, the PID controller, and the H-bridge or PWM driver supplying current to the
TEC. Indicate the feedback loop. This should be drawn specifically for your
hardware implementation.]

The system design details and measured thermal stability are described in
Section 3.3. The key objective is to reduce thermally induced drift below the
measurement noise floor, so that the ellipsometric spectra acquired represent a
stable, well-defined physical state.


2.7 Related Work and Literature Overview
──────────────────────────────────────────

The application of machine learning to spectroscopic ellipsometry has grown
substantially in the last five years. It is worth situating the present work within
that context carefully, because the claims made in Chapter 5 are only meaningful if
one understands what has and has not been done before.

The most directly foundational paper is Liu et al. (2021) [11], who framed SE
inversion as a supervised learning problem and showed that a neural network can
recover optical constants n, k, and thickness d from Ψ/Δ spectra. Their work was
published in Light: Science and Applications and has been widely cited. What they
did not do is study systematic noise effects or conduct wavelength range sweeps.

Arunachalam et al. (2022) [12] are the closest methodological parallel to this
work. They used a k-nearest-neighbour model trained on in situ SE data from ZnO
atomic layer deposition and found that good thickness prediction accuracy was
maintained even when 90% of the wavelength channels were discarded, a result
that the present Study 1 directly revisits and extends across multiple materials
and model types. Their work is single-material and single-seed, which limits the
generalisability of their results. A second paper from the same group [13] applied
a similar approach to TiO₂, achieving 88.76% accuracy within ±1.5 nm.

Kwak et al. (2021) [17] studied non-destructive thickness characterisation of 3D
multilayer semiconductor devices using optical spectra and artificial neural
networks. Their finding that ML is more robust to noisy measurements than analytical
fitting is the qualitative hypothesis that the present work tests quantitatively
with a structured noise study. Kwak and Kim (2023) [16] extended this to a broader
review of ML for semiconductor metrology, covering both ANN and convolutional
approaches for multi-stack geometries.

The EllipsoNet paper (Wang et al., 2022) [14] applied deep learning to ellipsometry
on unknown substrates, demonstrating that Kramers-Kronig relations were implicitly
learned by the network. This is methodologically interesting but addresses a more
complex problem than the present work.

At the more applied end, Barkhordari et al. (2024) [15] compared several classical
ML algorithms on real SE data for ZnTiO₃ nanocomposites, and Urban and Barton (2024)
[18] demonstrated neural network inversion for indium tin oxide on silicon. Both are
single-material studies.

The most recent work in the field, Oka et al. (2025) [19], uses deep learning to
simultaneously predict n, k, d, and the optical bandgap from SE data, representing
the current frontier. Ma et al. (2025) [23] provide a thorough review of inverse
design methods for optical thin films, from classical optimisation through to deep
generative models.

Against this backdrop, the present work contributes the following, to the best of
my knowledge:

      (i)   A simultaneous multi-material study covering five technologically
            important semiconductors under identical experimental conditions, rather
            than isolating one material per paper.

      (ii)  A dense, statistically averaged wavelength range sweep (91 cases, 5
            seeds) that provides a more complete picture of spectral data requirements
            than any prior single-seed study.

      (iii) A structured, five-type noise robustness study that directly compares
            ML and analytical inversion under controlled noise conditions.

      (iv)  A direct quantitative comparison of ML models against a physics-based
            analytical grid-search baseline, showing regimes where each approach
            is preferable.

3.1 Overall System Architecture
─────────────────────────────────

Before describing any individual component, it is useful to see the system as a
whole. The work presented in this thesis sits at the intersection of two domains:
optical measurement physics and data-driven machine learning. Designing a system
that spans both requires making deliberate choices at every stage, from the choice
of angle of incidence to the number of neurons in a hidden layer, and those choices
interact with each other in ways that are not always obvious at first glance. This
section maps out the full architecture so that individual design decisions in the
sections that follow can be understood in context.

The system has four main functional blocks. The first is the measurement front-end:
a spectroscopic ellipsometer that illuminates a semiconductor thin-film sample at a
fixed angle of incidence and records the polarisation change across a wavelength
range. This produces a pair of spectra, Ψ(λ) and Δ(λ), that encode the optical
properties and thickness of the film. The second block is the noise injection
layer, which is unique to this study. Because the work is carried out on synthetic
data, the measurement noise that a real instrument would introduce must be
explicitly modelled and added to the clean simulated spectra. Five noise models are
implemented, corresponding to physically motivated categories of instrument error.
The third block is the inversion layer, where either a machine learning model or a
physics-based analytical solver takes the (possibly noisy) spectrum as input and
produces a film thickness estimate as output. The fourth block is the evaluation
layer, where accuracy metrics are computed, aggregated across seeds and conditions,
and organised for analysis.

[FIGURE 3.1: Block diagram of the full system architecture. Draw four horizontally
connected blocks: (1) Optical Measurement Front-End showing SE instrument with
arrow labelled Ψ(λ), Δ(λ), (2) Noise Injection showing the five noise types as
bullet points flowing into the spectra, (3) Inversion Layer showing two parallel
paths, one for ML models (LR / RF / MLP) and one for the Analytical Grid-Search
Solver, (4) Evaluation showing RMSE, MAE, R² and the Study 1 / Study 2 arrows.
Include a feedback arrow from Evaluation back to Design for the wavelength sweep.]

The data flows in one direction: from simulation to evaluation. There is no active
feedback loop in the sense of iterative model updates in production, but there is
a study-level feedback in the experimental design: the results of Study 1 (wavelength
range sweep) directly inform whether a reduced-wavelength instrument could be built
without meaningful loss of accuracy. That connection between measurement system
design and ML outcome is one of the defining features of this work.


3.2 Optical Measurement System
────────────────────────────────

A spectroscopic ellipsometer operates by measuring the change in polarisation state
of light reflected from a surface. The basic hardware consists of five components:
a broadband light source, a polariser, the sample stage, an analyser, and a
detector. In the rotating-analyser configuration used as the reference in this work,
the polariser is fixed and the analyser rotates, sampling the reflected polarisation
state over a full rotation cycle. The raw signal is a sinusoidal intensity variation
as a function of analyser angle, from which the ellipsometric parameters Ψ and Δ
are extracted analytically. Modern commercial instruments digitise this at every
wavelength simultaneously using a charge-coupled device (CCD) or photodiode array,
so a full spectrum is acquired in seconds.

The angle of incidence is set to 70 degrees from the surface normal throughout this
work. This is not an arbitrary choice. For most semiconductors and dielectric films
in the 300–800 nm range, the Brewster angle falls somewhere between 65 and 75
degrees. Operating near the Brewster angle maximises the ellipsometric contrast —
that is, the sensitivity of Ψ and Δ to changes in film thickness and optical
constants, because the p-component reflection coefficient r_p passes through or
near zero in that region, amplifying the phase term Δ. At normal incidence, by
contrast, both polarisation components reflect almost identically, Δ approaches
zero or 180 degrees, and the measurement yields little useful information about the
film. The 70-degree configuration is therefore the standard engineering trade-off
between sensitivity and practical accessibility of the sample.

The wavelength range covered is 300 to 800 nm, sampled at every integer nanometre,
giving 501 spectral points per measurement. This range was chosen to include the
near-UV region where most of the semiconductor materials studied here are strongly
absorbing (large k), as well as the visible and near-IR regions where absorption
drops and transparency increases. The full 501-point spectrum is used as the feature
vector for machine learning. Study 1, described in Chapter 4, will progressively
reduce this range to examine how much spectral content is actually needed for
accurate inversion.

The sample in this work is a semiconductor thin film on an SiO₂ substrate. The
substrate is optically thick, far thicker than the coherence length of the light
source, so back-surface reflections do not contribute to the measurement and the
three-layer air/film/substrate model applies without modification. Film thicknesses
range from 20 to 80 nm. Below 20 nm, the films become thin enough that surface
roughness and interface contributions would need additional modelling. Above 80 nm,
the spectra start to show rapidly oscillating interference fringes that complicate
the regression problem without adding methodological insight. The 20 to 80 nm range
is typical for gate oxides, passivation layers, and anti-reflection coatings on
process-monitor wafers.


3.3 Temperature Control System (Peltier Module)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────

3.3.1 Peltier Module and Actuator Hardware

Thermal stability of the sample stage is a practical requirement that is often
treated as secondary in simulation-focused studies but becomes critical the moment
real hardware enters the measurement chain. As noted in Section 2.6, the refractive
indices of semiconductors shift by approximately 1 to 2 × 10^-4 K^-1 in the visible
range for silicon, and somewhat more for direct-gap materials such as GaAs and GaN
near their band edges. Over a measurement dwell time of one to two minutes in an
uncontrolled laboratory environment, ambient temperature drifts of 1 to 2 K are
routine, and the resulting shift in optical constants translates into a systematic
thickness error of one nanometre or more. That is not a tolerable contribution when
the target accuracy of the ML models in this work is in the 1.5 to 2 nm range.

Existing thermoelectric temperature control systems in the literature predominantly
rely on bidirectional current, which allows the TEC to both heat and cool the
controlled surface symmetrically. This bidirectional capability simplifies PID
tuning considerably: if the controller overshoots the setpoint in one direction, it
can immediately apply current in the opposite direction to correct. The system
designed in this work deliberately departs from that convention. For reasons of
cost, electrical simplicity, and the constraints of the chosen Keysight DC supply
operating in a single-polarity mode, the TEC is driven unidirectionally, cooling
only. The current can be reduced to zero, allowing the stage to passively warm via
environmental heat load, but it cannot be reversed to actively heat.

This constraint introduces three control challenges that have received little
attention in the literature for optical metrology applications. First, the response
is asymmetric: cooling is fast (a TEC removes heat actively) but warming is slow
(the stage must rely on ambient heat flux through the thermal interface). Second,
overshoot below the setpoint cannot be corrected by active heating, so any
downward overshoot must be recovered passively and is limited in recovery speed by
the thermal mass of the stage and the ambient temperature margin. Third, the system
is operated close to the dew point, a regime that conventional bidirectional TEC
systems avoid, which means the overshoot risk carries a direct measurement
consequence: a cold-side temperature that dips below the dew point even briefly
induces condensation on the sample surface, corrupting the ellipsometric measurement
irrecoverably for that cycle.

Addressing all three challenges within a low-cost, microcontroller-based
implementation is the central engineering contribution of the temperature control
subsystem in this thesis. The solution strategy, detailed in Section 3.3.3 and
Section 4.6, combines a conservative open-loop ramp phase, asymmetry-aware PID
tuning, integrator anti-windup specifically configured for one-sided saturation,
and a dew-point interlock that enforces a hard lower bound on the control signal.

The actuator is a Peltier thermoelectric cooler (TEC) module. A TEC is a solid-state
heat pump with no moving parts: when a direct current flows across junctions of
dissimilar conductive materials, the Peltier effect transports heat from one face
of the device (the cold side) to the opposite face (the hot side). The thermal power
balance at the cold side is

      Q_c = α · T_c · I  −  (1/2) · I² · R  −  K · ΔT            ...(3.1)

where α is the Seebeck coefficient of the thermoelectric material, I is the applied
current, R is the module's electrical resistance, K is the thermal conductance
between the two faces, and ΔT = T_h − T_c is the temperature difference across the
module. The first term represents Peltier cooling, the second Joule heating within
the module itself, and the third the parasitic back-conduction of heat from the hot
side to the cold side. In practical terms, equation (3.1) sets the operating limits:
for a given cold-side target temperature there is a maximum current beyond which
Joule heating dominates and the cold side actually warms up.

Electrical actuation of the Peltier module is provided by a Keysight programmable
DC power supply, which allows precise, software-controlled adjustment of the applied
voltage and current. The supply communicates with the control computer via a standard
interface, enabling the control algorithm to update the setpoint in real time and to
log the instantaneous voltage and current alongside the temperature data. Using a
bench-grade power supply rather than a custom H-bridge driver eliminates a significant
source of electrical noise that would otherwise couple into the measurement electronics
through the shared ground plane.

3.3.2 Sensing Instrumentation

Two complementary sensing strategies are employed to monitor the thermal state of
the system. The primary measurement of sample-stage temperature is performed by a
PT100 platinum resistance thermometer embedded in the sample holder directly above
the cold side of the TEC. PT100 sensors exhibit a well-characterised and nearly
linear resistance-temperature relationship, with a nominal resistance of 100 Ω at
0 °C and a sensitivity of approximately 0.385 Ω K^-1. The resolution achievable
with a PT100 read through a precision interface is in the millikelvin range, which
far exceeds the ±0.1 K stability target set for this system.

Interfacing a PT100 to a microcontroller requires an amplified resistance measurement
front end. The MAX31865 RTD-to-digital converter is used here. This integrated
circuit drives the sensor with a precision current source, measures the resulting
voltage across the PT100 using a 15-bit analogue-to-digital converter, and
communicates the result to the host microcontroller via a four-wire SPI bus. The
MAX31865 handles the reference resistance ratio internally, reducing the sensitivity
to cable resistance errors that would otherwise corrupt a simple voltage-divider
measurement, an important consideration since the sensor wiring runs close to the
optical stage and must be screened.

Secondary environmental monitoring is provided by a DHT22 combined temperature and
relative humidity sensor mounted away from the sample stage but within the instrument
enclosure. The DHT22 measures ambient air conditions at a stated accuracy of ±0.5 K
in temperature and ±2–5 % in relative humidity. Its primary function in this system
is not to control temperature but to flag conditions near the dew point. If the
surface temperature of the cold side falls below the local dew point, condensation
on the sample or on optical windows alters the measured Ψ and Δ immediately and
irreversibly for that measurement cycle. The DHT22 reading is therefore used as an
interlock: closed-loop temperature setpoints below the current dew-point temperature,
computed from the DHT22 readings, are rejected by the control software with a warning.

The Arduino Uno microcontroller board serves as the central interface hub. It reads
the PT100 temperature via SPI from the MAX31865, polls the DHT22 over its single-wire
protocol, executes the PID control algorithm in the main loop, and sends the
computed control signal as a setpoint word to the Keysight supply over serial. The
loop runs at approximately 10 Hz, which is well above the thermal bandwidth of the
sample stage (time constant of several seconds) and therefore does not introduce
any aliasing or latency-related instability.

3.3.3 PID Control Architecture

The control objective is to drive the error signal

      e(t) = T_ref − T(t)                                           ...(3.2)

to zero, where T_ref is the desired setpoint and T(t) is the PT100 reading at time t.
The control action, expressed as the voltage command u(t) sent to the Keysight
power supply, is computed using a standard PID formulation

      u(t) = K_p · e(t)  +  K_i · ∫₀ᵗ e(τ) dτ  +  K_d · de(t)/dt  ...(3.3)

The proportional term K_p · e(t) provides an immediate response proportional to the
present deviation. It drives the system toward the setpoint quickly but, on its own,
leaves a residual steady-state error for any finite load. The integral term
K_i · ∫e dt accumulates the history of the error and eliminates this steady-state
offset. It also compensates for slow environmental disturbances such as a gradual
rise in ambient temperature during a long measurement session. The derivative term
K_d · de/dt reacts to the rate of change of error, damping oscillations that would
otherwise occur if the proportional gain alone were increased to achieve faster
settling.

The unidirectional constraint changes the practical interpretation of equation (3.3)
fundamentally. In a bidirectional system, u(t) can be positive or negative and the
physical plant responds to both. Here, u(t) is effectively lower-bounded at zero:
when the controller computes a positive command (i.e. heat is needed), the only
option is to reduce the cooling current and wait for the ambient heat flux to warm
the stage. The positive half of the u(t) axis is therefore the slow, passive regime,
and the negative half is the fast, active cooling regime. This creates an intrinsic
asymmetry in closed-loop bandwidth: the cooling time constant is set by TEC
performance (order of seconds), while the warming time constant is set by the thermal
mass of the stage divided by the passive heat flux from the environment (order of
tens of seconds or more, depending on ambient conditions).

The PID gains are tuned to account for this asymmetry. The proportional gain K_p is
deliberately kept moderate to limit the depth of downward overshoot. Since the system
cannot actively recover from a cold overshoot near the dew point, a conservative K_p
that accepts slightly slower approach in exchange for shallower undershoot is
preferable to an aggressive gain that cools quickly but risks condensation. The
derivative term plays a correspondingly larger role: K_d is increased relative to a
standard symmetric system, so that the deceleration of the cooling rate is initiated
early and the cold-side temperature approaches T_ref with a low net velocity.

In the implemented system, saturation limits are imposed on u(t). The Keysight
supply voltage is clamped to a safe operating range for the chosen TEC module,
preventing excessive current that would shift the operating point into the Joule-
heating-dominated regime described by equation (3.1). Anti-windup logic is applied
to the integrator with a one-sided asymmetric scheme: the integrator is frozen only
when the output saturates at the lower bound (zero volts), not at the upper bound.
This prevents the integrator from accumulating a large positive value during a
passive warming phase, which would cause the system to over-cool aggressively once
cooling is re-engaged, creating repeated undershoot cycles near the dew point.

Start-up is handled by a two-phase strategy. During the initial open-loop phase,
the supply voltage is ramped up incrementally to bring the cold-side temperature
close to the setpoint without overshooting. Once the PT100 reading enters a
tolerance band of ±0.5 K above T_ref (strictly from above, given the cooling-only
constraint), the Arduino switches to closed-loop PID operation. This hybrid strategy
reduces settling time and avoids the integrator windup that would accumulate during
the ramp-up phase if the integrator were active from the start.

3.3.4 Measurement Stability and Thermo-Optic Effects

The design target for the closed-loop system is temperature stability within ±0.1 K
over the duration of a single ellipsometric sweep. To understand why this is the
right specification, consider the two dominant pathways through which temperature
affects the measured Ψ and Δ: refractive index variation and thermal expansion.

The refractive index of an optical material depends on temperature through the
thermo-optic coefficient dn/dT. To first order,

      n(T) = n_0 + (dn/dT) · (T − T_0)                            ...(3.4)

where n_0 is the index at reference temperature T_0. For silicon in the near-visible
range, dn/dT ≈ 1.8 × 10^-4 K^-1. A temperature excursion of ±0.1 K therefore shifts
n by ±1.8 × 10^-5, a change that is sub-dominant to the other noise contributions
characterised in Chapter 4 of this work. For GaAs and GaN near their band edges the
thermo-optic coefficients are larger, typically (2–4) × 10^-4 K^-1, but the ±0.1 K
target still keeps the resulting index error at or below 4 × 10^-5, which remains
tolerable.

The second pathway is thermal expansion. A temperature change ΔT modifies the
physical film thickness according to

      Δd = α_T · d · (T − T_0)                                     ...(3.5)

where α_T is the linear coefficient of thermal expansion of the film. For a 50 nm
silicon film with α_T ≈ 2.6 × 10^-6 K^-1, a 1 K change produces a thickness shift
of only 0.00013 nm, which is entirely negligible. Thermal expansion therefore
contributes less than the refractive index pathway by several orders of magnitude,
and the ±0.1 K specification is driven entirely by the optical-constant requirement.

The DHT22-based dew-point interlock adds a further layer of protection. In a
laboratory environment where the cold-side setpoint may approach 15–18 °C during
testing, the dew-point margin can become small on humid days. A condensation event
would cause an apparent spike in Δ of tens of degrees, an obvious outlier, but one
that could corrupt a batch measurement without a software guard.

3.3.5 System Integration

Mechanically, the TEC sits beneath the sample holder on the ellipsometer stage. The
cold side is in thermal contact with the underside of the sample holder through a
thin layer of thermal interface material, and the hot side is pressed against an
aluminium heat sink fitted with a small brushless fan. The PT100 sensor, connected
to the MAX31865, is embedded in a small pocket milled into the edge of the sample
holder, placing it within 5 mm of the sample position. The DHT22 is fixed to the
inside of the enclosure wall, measuring ambient air rather than the stage itself.

The Arduino Uno is mounted externally, connected by a short USB cable for serial
communication with the control PC. The MAX31865 is wired to the Arduino SPI pins
(MOSI, MISO, SCK, CS). The DHT22 uses a single data wire connected to a digital
input with a pull-up resistor. All low-level sensor wiring is screened and routed
away from the optical path to avoid stray light and electromagnetic interference
with the ellipsometer detector chain.

3.4 Selection of Semiconductor Materials and Substrate
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The choice of which semiconductor materials to include determines the breadth of
the training problem. A model trained only on silicon is a silicon-specific tool.
A model trained across five materials with very different optical properties is a
general-purpose inversion engine, but it also faces a harder regression problem
because the input space is far more varied.

Five materials were selected: silicon (Si), gallium arsenide (GaAs), gallium nitride
(GaN), germanium (Ge), and indium phosphide (InP). The selection was guided by
three criteria: technological relevance, optical diversity, and availability of
well-characterised experimental optical constants.

Silicon is the foundation of the microelectronics industry and the most widely
measured semiconductor in ellipsometric studies. It is indirect-bandgap with
moderate absorption in the visible. Its optical constants are tabulated with high
accuracy by Green (2008) [2], who provides values at room temperature from multiple
experimental techniques. Including silicon is non-negotiable.

Germanium is a group-IV semiconductor like silicon but with a substantially smaller
bandgap (0.67 eV versus 1.12 eV for Si at room temperature). This means Ge absorbs
strongly across the entire 300–800 nm range, including the near-IR, and its k values
are large throughout. The Ψ and Δ spectra of a Ge film look qualitatively different
from silicon, which tests whether a single ML model can handle both cases. Optical
constants follow Aspnes and Studna (1983) [1].

Gallium arsenide is the most important III-V compound semiconductor for photonics
and high-frequency electronics. Its direct bandgap at 1.42 eV places its band edge
at about 870 nm, just outside our wavelength range, so the material is strongly
absorbing below about 870 nm and transparent above. Within the 300–800 nm window,
GaAs shows a rich spectral structure with strong critical-point features near 3 eV
(~413 nm) and 4.5 eV (~275 nm). These features produce sharp, structured variation
in n and k that is very different from the smoother behaviour of silicon. This
spectral richness makes GaAs a challenging and informative test case.

Indium phosphide has a direct bandgap at 1.35 eV, placing it slightly below GaAs
in energy. Its optical properties are somewhat smoother than GaAs in the visible
range, making it a useful intermediate case. InP is the substrate material of
choice for long-wavelength (1.3–1.55 µm) photonic integrated circuits, and thin-
film characterisation of epilayers on InP substrates is a routine industrial problem
[24]. Optical constants follow Aspnes and Studna (1983) [1].

Gallium nitride is a wide-bandgap semiconductor (3.4 eV, corresponding to about
365 nm) used in blue and UV LEDs, high-power RF transistors, and power electronics
[3]. Because its bandgap falls within the 300–800 nm window, the material transitions
from strongly absorbing at short wavelengths (near its band edge) to relatively
transparent at longer wavelengths. This transparency-to-absorption transition produces
distinctive spectral features in both n and k that no other material in this set
replicates. GaN optical constants are taken from Kawashima et al. (1997) [3] via
the Polyanskiy database [5].

The substrate throughout is thermally grown silicon dioxide (SiO₂). SiO₂ is
transparent across the entire 300–800 nm range and well-characterised. Its refractive
index was modelled using the Sellmeier equation with Malitson (1965) [4] coefficients.
As a substrate material, SiO₂ is also broadly representative of process-monitor wafers
used in real semiconductor manufacturing, where thin films are routinely deposited
on oxide layers for electrical isolation purposes.

Together, these five materials span a wide range of optical properties: from Ge with
strong, smooth absorption across the full window, through Si and GaAs with structured
features in the visible, to the near-transparent GaN and InP at long wavelengths.
This diversity is intentional. A machine learning model that can predict thickness
accurately across all five simultaneously, with a single set of weights and a single
training procedure, is far more general and practically useful than five separate
single-material models.


3.5 Selection of Machine Learning Models
──────────────────────────────────────────

Three models were selected to represent a deliberate range of complexity and
interpretability: Linear Regression, Random Forest Regression, and a Multilayer
Perceptron neural network. The selection was not motivated by a prior preference for
any particular approach but by the scientific question the comparison is meant to
answer.

Linear Regression was included precisely because it is expected to fail. With 1,002
input features (Ψ and Δ at 501 wavelengths each) and only around 244 training samples
in the smallest dataset size cases, the problem is grossly underdetermined. A linear
model cannot meaningfully fit in this regime without regularisation, and none is
applied here, so the results represent a genuine lower bound on regression performance.
This is useful information: it quantifies how much is gained by moving to a nonlinear
model. Without this baseline, the Random Forest and MLP results have less context.

Random Forest was chosen for two reasons: predictive performance and interpretability.
Ensemble averaging across 100 trees reduces variance substantially compared to any
single decision tree, and the model generalises well in high-dimensional input spaces
with relatively few training samples, which is precisely the regime this study occupies
in its smaller dataset conditions [21][22]. The second reason is the Gini feature
importance score, which provides a direct, wavelength-resolved measure of which
spectral channels contribute most to the thickness prediction. This is not available
from the MLP in any convenient form, and it turns out to be one of the most
physically informative results in the entire study.

The Multilayer Perceptron is the representationally most powerful of the three. With
three hidden layers of 256, 128, and 64 neurons and ReLU activations, the network
can approximate highly nonlinear functions and, in principle, learn the full
complexity of the mapping from a 1,002-dimensional spectrum to a single thickness
value. The architecture was chosen based on preliminary experiments that showed
this configuration converges reliably and does not overfit badly with the dataset
sizes used here, particularly when combined with a validation split for early stopping.
More complex architectures, such as convolutional networks treating the spectrum as
a one-dimensional signal, were deliberately not used: the goal was not to achieve
the maximum possible accuracy, but to study how a representative neural network
performs relative to classical methods under the study conditions.

The deliberate choice to keep all three models in their standard scikit-learn
implementations, without specialised hyperparameter tuning per condition, is also
worth noting. Tuning each model for each noise type and wavelength range would
produce better numbers but would make the study design far more complex and the
results harder to interpret. Holding the model configurations fixed allows clean
attribution: differences in performance across conditions are due to the conditions,
not to the models being better or worse tuned.


3.6 Software and Data Processing Pipeline
───────────────────────────────────────────

The entire computational pipeline is implemented in Python 3, using standard
scientific computing libraries throughout. NumPy handles all array operations and
complex arithmetic for the Fresnel simulations. Pandas organises the dataset as a
wide-format DataFrame, with one row per (material, thickness) combination and 1,002
feature columns. Scikit-learn (version 1.x) provides all three ML models, the
train/test split, preprocessing, and metric computation. Matplotlib generates all
figures.

The pipeline proceeds in a fixed sequence. First, the forward simulation runs for
all five materials across all thickness values and all 501 wavelengths, producing the
clean dataset. This takes a few seconds on a modern laptop. Second, the five noise
datasets are generated from the clean dataset by applying the noise functions
described in Section 4.2. Each noise dataset has exactly the same structure as the
clean dataset and is saved independently as a CSV file. Third, the ML studies run:
for each combination of (noise type, wavelength range, dataset size, seed), a model
is trained and evaluated, and the metrics are stored. Fourth, the analytical solver
runs independently on each condition, with no training phase, directly minimising
the chi-squared cost function for each test spectrum. Fifth, diagnostics are
computed: residual distributions and feature importance scores for the clean-data
case.

Data are not normalised before being written to the CSV files. Input feature
standardisation (zero mean, unit variance) is applied internally within the MLP
preprocessing step only, so it is always computed from the training split and applied
to both train and test without leakage. The Random Forest and Linear Regression
models receive raw feature values, which is appropriate since neither is
distance-sensitive in the same way as gradient-based methods.

The software components are organised into numbered directories corresponding to
the thesis chapters: 2_simulation for the forward model and noise generation,
4_ml_initial and subsequent folders for the ML studies, 8_analytical_baseline for
the grid-search solver, and 9_diagnostics for the post-hoc analyses. Each directory
is a self-contained Python script that reads its input data from a fixed relative
path and writes its outputs to a results subdirectory. Running the pipeline from
scratch requires executing the scripts in directory order. The entire study, from
simulation to final diagnostics, completes in under an hour on a standard laptop
CPU, with the Random Forest studies being the most computationally intensive step.

4.1 Synthetic Dataset Generation (Fresnel Forward Simulation)
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The foundation of everything that follows is a synthetic dataset built by
numerically evaluating the Fresnel thin-film forward model described in Chapter 2.
Because no real experimental instrument was available for this study, the simulation
serves as the ground truth: it defines both the training data for the ML models and
the reference measurements against which all predictions are evaluated. Using
synthetic data has a practical consequence that is worth stating explicitly, it
means the results are free from uncontrolled experimental artefacts, instrument
drift, and alignment errors, but it also means they describe an idealised physical
scenario. The relationship between synthetic performance and real-instrument
performance is a question for future work.

The simulation is implemented in the script 2_simulation/main.py. The configuration
is fixed: angle of incidence 70 degrees, wavelengths from 300 to 800 nm in 1 nm
steps (501 points), and film thicknesses from 20 to 80 nm in 1 nm steps (61 values).
Across five semiconductor materials, this produces a total of 501 × 61 × 5 = 152,805
simulation runs. Each run evaluates the full Airy thin-film interference formula for
both polarisation states independently, using complex-valued Fresnel coefficients
throughout.

The optical constants for each material are loaded from pre-tabulated CSV files at
every wavelength, so dispersive effects are fully accounted for. For each
(material, thickness, wavelength) triplet, the calculation proceeds as follows. The
refraction angle in the film is computed using Snell's law in complex form:

      θ_1 = arcsin(N_0 · sin θ_0 / N_1)                              ...(4.1)

The Fresnel reflection coefficients at the air/film interface (subscript 01) and at
the film/substrate interface (subscript 12) are then computed for both polarisation
states using equations (2.4) and (2.5). The phase thickness accumulated during a
single traversal of the film is:

      β = (2π / λ) · N_1 · d · cos θ_1                               ...(4.2)

The total reflection amplitude for each polarisation, accounting for the infinite
series of multiple reflections inside the film, follows from the Airy formula
(equation 2.8). Once r_total,p and r_total,s are obtained, the ellipsometric
angles are extracted as:

      Ψ = arctan(|r_p / r_s|)    (converted to degrees)
      Δ = arg(r_p / r_s)         (converted to degrees)

These two numbers are stored as a row in the output CSV, along with the material
label, film thickness, and wavelength. The full long-format output has five columns:
material, thickness_nm, wavelength_nm, psi_deg, delta_deg. There are 152,805 rows
in total.

The reason for working in long format at this stage is flexibility: it is easier to
filter by wavelength range or downsample by thickness at the pivoting step than to
keep the data permanently wide. Conversion to wide format, one row per
(material, thickness) pair, with 1,002 feature columns for the 501 Ψ and 501 Δ
values, is done inside the ML training scripts at run time. The long-format CSV is
the single source of truth.

[FIGURE 4.1: Example simulated spectra for all five materials at a representative
thickness, e.g. d = 50 nm. Two subplots side by side: Ψ(λ) on the left and Δ(λ)
on the right. Overlay all five materials with distinct line colours. This shows the
reader the diversity of the training data and motivates why the multi-material
problem is harder than a single-material one.]

The main simulation runs in a few seconds on a laptop. No parallelisation was
needed. The output file, ellipsometry_dataset.csv, is the input for every
subsequent step of the pipeline.


4.2 Noise Modelling
─────────────────────

Five noise models are implemented in 2_simulation/noise.py. Each one reads the
clean dataset, applies the relevant perturbation to the psi_deg and delta_deg
columns, wraps the Delta values back into the range [-180°, 180°] to maintain
physical consistency, and saves the result as a separate CSV. The five output files
share the exact same column structure as the clean dataset and are called
dataset_gaussian_noise.csv, dataset_relative_noise.csv, dataset_uniform_noise.csv,
dataset_bias_noise.csv, and dataset_mixed_noise.csv.

The wrapping step deserves a brief comment. Delta is a phase angle and is periodic
with period 360 degrees. Adding noise to a Delta value that sits near the +180° or
-180° boundary can push it outside the physical range. The wrap operation:

      Δ_wrapped = (Δ + 180°) mod 360° − 180°                         ...(4.3)

keeps all values in [-180°, 180°] without altering the underlying phase information.
This is applied after every noise addition operation and to both noisy and clean
Delta values as a consistency check.

Gaussian noise adds an independent zero-mean normal draw to each Ψ and Δ value at
each wavelength point. The standard deviation is 0.5 degrees. The independence
across wavelengths is deliberate: shot noise in a CCD detector is spectrally
uncorrelated because each pixel receives a statistically independent photon count.
The noise is redrawn independently for each row in the dataset, so every
(material, thickness) spectrum receives a different noise realisation.

Relative noise multiplies each Ψ and Δ value by a factor drawn from a uniform
distribution over [1 − α, 1 + α] with α = 0.01 (one percent). The draw is
independent at each wavelength. This represents a multiplicative noise source —
intensity fluctuations that scale with signal amplitude, rather than an additive
one. The practical difference is that relative noise is larger where Ψ and Δ are
large, and smaller where they are small, which produces a qualitatively different
error pattern across the spectrum.

Uniform noise adds a draw from U(−1°, +1°) to each data point. Relative to
Gaussian noise (σ = 0.5°), uniform noise at ±1° is wider by a factor of about 1.73
in standard deviation terms and has a flat probability density rather than a peaked
one. It provides a worst-case symmetric bound. In practice, real noise distributions
are rarely uniform. The model is included because it is the standard comparison
partner to Gaussian in robustness studies.

Systematic bias adds a fixed constant of +0.5 degrees to every Ψ and Δ value at
every wavelength. Unlike the random noise types, the perturbation is identical for
every row and every wavelength within a measurement. This is the hardest type of
noise for ML to handle, because it does not average out across samples or wavelengths.
A model trained on clean data will see a consistent offset between training and test
data, with no within-sample variance to hint at the direction of the bias.

Mixed noise superimposes Gaussian (σ = 0.5°) and bias (+0.5°) simultaneously, so
the total perturbation at each wavelength is:

      ε_mixed(λ) = ε_G(λ) + b,     ε_G ~ N(0, 0.25),   b = +0.5°   ...(4.4)

This is the most physically realistic model: a well-used instrument typically has
residual calibration offsets (bias) alongside unavoidable random fluctuations.

One implementation note: the noise in these datasets is applied at the long-format
level (one row per wavelength point) rather than at the spectrum level. For
Gaussian, relative, and uniform noise, each wavelength point within a spectrum gets
an independent draw, which is the physically correct behaviour for spectrally
uncorrelated noise. For bias noise, every point in the file gets the same constant
added, which is also correct, bias is a global instrument offset, not a per-
wavelength random effect.


4.3 Machine Learning Pipeline
───────────────────────────────

The ML pipeline is implemented in 6_ml_averaged/ml_averaged.py. The script loads
the clean ellipsometry dataset, pivots it to wide format, and then executes two
studies in sequence. The same pipeline structure, with the clean dataset replaced
by a noise variant, is used in 7_ml_noise/ml_noise.py for the noise robustness
study.

Dataset Pivoting. The long-format dataset (152,805 rows × 5 columns) is pivoted
into wide format using a two-step procedure. First, the psi_deg values are pivoted
so that each row corresponds to a unique (material, thickness) pair and each column
corresponds to one wavelength. This produces a 305 × 501 matrix for the Ψ channels.
The same operation is applied to delta_deg, yielding a second 305 × 501 matrix for
the Δ channels. The two matrices are concatenated horizontally, giving a final
feature matrix of 305 rows by 1,002 columns. Column names follow the convention
psi_300, psi_301, ..., psi_800, delta_300, ..., delta_800. The target vector is
the thickness_nm column, extracted before pivoting.

Train/Test Split. All experiments use an 80/20 stratified train/test split, where
stratification is by material label. With 305 total samples (61 thicknesses × 5
materials), the split gives 244 training samples and 61 test samples. Stratification
ensures that all five materials appear in both the training and test set with equal
proportions, otherwise, with only 61 samples per material, unlucky splits could
exclude a material from training entirely. The split is controlled by a random seed
argument, and every experiment is run under seeds [42, 0, 1, 7, 13] sequentially.

Wavelength Subsetting. For Study 1, wavelength subsets of size n_wl are constructed
by selecting n_wl evenly-spaced indices from the full 501-wavelength array using
numpy.linspace, then rounding to integer positions. This preserves the approximate
spectral coverage at all subset sizes rather than truncating from one end, which
would bias the feature set toward UV or IR. At n_wl = 501, all columns are used.
At n_wl = 50, 50 evenly-spaced wavelengths are selected across the full 300–800 nm
range, with both Ψ and Δ columns for those wavelengths, giving a 100-column feature
matrix.

Model Training. Three models are trained in each experiment. Linear Regression
uses the sklearn.linear_model.LinearRegression implementation with default settings:
no regularisation, ordinary least-squares solution. Random Forest uses
sklearn.ensemble.RandomForestRegressor with 100 trees, n_jobs=-1 for parallelism
across CPU cores, and a seed-controlled random state. The MLP uses
sklearn.neural_network.MLPRegressor with hidden_layer_sizes=(256, 128, 64),
activation='relu', max_iter=2000, early_stopping=True with a 10% validation
fraction, and the L-BFGS solver (the default for small datasets). Before the MLP
is trained, the feature matrix is standardised to zero mean and unit variance using
sklearn.preprocessing.StandardScaler fitted on the training split only. This
prevents the test set from influencing the scaling and eliminates a common source
of data leakage.

The Random Forest and Linear Regression models receive unstandardised feature
values. This is intentional: decision trees are invariant to monotonic feature
transformations, so standardisation would change nothing for RF. For LR, the
unstandardised results are appropriate for comparison with the theoretical
underdetermined floor. Regularisation and standardisation would unfairly improve LR
relative to its raw performance.

Result Aggregation. After all five seeds are evaluated for a given (model, condition)
pair, the five RMSE values, five MAE values, and five R² values are averaged to
produce a mean and a standard deviation. These are the numbers reported throughout
Chapter 5. The standard deviation across seeds quantifies how sensitive the result
is to the random choice of train/test split and model initialisation. A small
standard deviation indicates a stable, reliable estimate. A large one suggests that
the particular split matters, which is usually a sign that the dataset is small
relative to the problem complexity.


4.4 Analytical Inverse Solver (Lookup Table Grid Search)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The analytical baseline solver is implemented in 8_analytical_baseline/
analytical_inverse.py. It uses no training data and no statistical model. Given a
test spectrum (Ψ_meas(λ), Δ_meas(λ)) from a known material, it predicts the film
thickness by finding the thickness on a pre-computed grid that minimises the sum of
squared residuals between the measured and model spectra.

The implementation proceeds in two phases. In the first phase, a lookup table is
built for each material. For a given material with known complex refractive index
array N_1(λ) and the fixed substrate N_2(λ), the forward model is evaluated
simultaneously over all 501 wavelengths and all 1,491 grid thicknesses in a single
vectorised NumPy operation. This is efficient because all the Fresnel operations
can be expressed as element-wise array arithmetic with broadcasting over both
the wavelength and thickness dimensions. The result is a lookup table of shape
(1491, 501, 2), thickness grid points × wavelengths × (Ψ, Δ). Building the
lookup table for all five materials takes a few seconds.

The thickness grid spans from 1.0 nm to 150.0 nm in steps of 0.1 nm, giving 1,491
candidate values. The step size of 0.1 nm is fine enough that the best-fit
thickness is not meaningfully limited by grid resolution: the Ψ/Δ sensitivity to
thickness is continuous and well-behaved over this range, and the contribution of
grid-step error to the reported RMSE is far below 0.1 nm on average.

In the second phase, each test sample is processed individually. For a test sample
from material m with measured spectrum (Ψ_meas, Δ_meas), the chi-squared cost
function is evaluated over all grid points:

      χ²(d) = Σ_λ [ (Ψ_model(d,λ) − Ψ_meas(λ))² + (Δ_model(d,λ) − Δ_meas(λ))² ]
                                                                       ...(4.5)

where the model values come directly from the lookup table. Because the lookup table
is already in memory as a NumPy array, this sum-of-squared-residuals calculation is
a single vectorised subtraction and sum operation, i.e. no Python loop over
wavelengths. The predicted thickness is then argmin χ²(d) over the grid. The whole
evaluation loop over the test set runs in under a minute.

The solver is completely deterministic and has no tunable hyperparameters. It makes
only one assumption beyond the forward model: that the material identity is known.
In a real measurement scenario, the material is typically known from the process
context. This assumption is shared with the ML models, which are also trained and
tested with the material label as implicit context (through the stratified split).

An important design note: the test samples fed to the analytical solver are taken
from the same 20% test split used for the ML models. This guarantees a fair
comparison, both the analytical and ML predictions are evaluated on the same set
of (spectrum, true thickness) pairs, and neither method has seen those particular
spectra during any training or lookup-table-construction phase. For the noise
studies, the solver receives the noisy versions of the test spectra (from the
corresponding noise dataset) for each of the five noise conditions.


4.5 Evaluation Framework (Study 1 and Study 2)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────

The two parameter studies that form the core of the ML evaluation are both
implemented within the main ML training script. They are designed as independent
sweeps so that each one isolates a single design variable while holding everything
else constant.

Study 1: Wavelength Range Sweep. The full 501-point spectrum is progressively
subsampled from 501 wavelengths down to 50 wavelengths, in steps of 5 (from 495
downward), plus the endpoint at 501. This gives 91 distinct cases. At each case,
the evenly-spaced wavelength selection described in Section 4.3 determines which
columns of the feature matrix are used. All other parameters remain fixed: the
same full dataset (305 samples), the same 80/20 stratified split, the same three
models, and the same five seeds. The output of Study 1 is a curve of RMSE versus
number of wavelength points for each model, showing how quickly accuracy degrades
as spectral information is removed.

The practical motivation is direct. A commercial spectroscopic ellipsometer
measuring at 501 wavelengths is a significantly more expensive instrument than one
measuring at, say, 100 wavelengths, because a wider spectral range requires either
more detector pixels or a wider-bandwidth light source and optics. If the RMSE
curve flattens out at some point below 501, it implies that a narrower-bandwidth
instrument could deliver equivalent accuracy at lower cost. The wavelength sweep
quantifies exactly where that plateau is.

Study 2: Training Dataset Size Sweep. The number of distinct thickness values
included in the training set is reduced from 61 down to 1, in steps of 1, giving
61 distinct cases. At each step, the n_thick most densely-sampled thickness values
are retained (the thick values closest to the set [20, 21, ..., 80] nm are selected
using even spacing over the available range), and the feature matrix is reconstructed
from the corresponding rows. The test set always comes from the same full 305-sample
split, so the evaluation is always over the complete set of thicknesses even when
training on a small subset.

The degradation of accuracy as training data shrinks tells us the minimum number
of calibration measurements needed to train a reliable model. This is relevant for
practical deployment: generating training data experimentally is expensive, as each
(material, thickness) training point requires depositing and characterising a real
sample. If the model generalises well from 20 distinct thicknesses, the calibration
burden is manageable. If it requires 50 or 60 distinct values, it may not be
practical without automation.

Total Computational Cost. Across both studies for a single noise condition:
Study 1 requires 91 wavelength cases × 3 models × 5 seeds = 1,365 model fits.
Study 2 requires 61 dataset-size cases × 3 models × 5 seeds = 915 model fits.
The combined total is 2,280 model fits per noise condition. Adding the five noise
conditions to the clean baseline gives a total of 6 × 2,280 = 13,680 model fits
across the full experiment, plus the analytical solver runs and diagnostics.
Despite this scale, the entire pipeline runs to completion in under an hour on a
standard laptop, because each individual fit (particularly LR and RF on a dataset
of 305 rows) takes milliseconds. The MLP training is the slowest step, but with
early stopping and max_iter = 2,000, even the heaviest MLP fits converge quickly.


4.6 Temperature Control System. Hardware Implementation
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The temperature regulation subsystem described at the design level in Section 3.3 is
realised in hardware using four main components: a PT100 platinum resistance
thermometer, a MAX31865 precision RTD interface, a DHT22 ambient sensor, and a
Keysight programmable DC power supply, all orchestrated by an Arduino Uno
microcontroller. This section describes how these components are connected and how
the control software maps the design equations onto embedded code running within the
Arduino loop.

4.6.1 Sensor Acquisition

The PT100 is the primary measurement element. Its resistance changes by approximately
0.385 Ω K^-1 from a nominal 100 Ω at 0 °C. Resolving millikelvin-level changes
requires a front-end circuit with a stable excitation source and a high-resolution
analogue-to-digital converter. The MAX31865 provides both: it drives the PT100
with a precision current source, takes a ratiometric 15-bit measurement relative to
a 430 Ω reference resistor, and delivers the result over a four-wire SPI bus. On
the Arduino side, the SPI pins (MOSI, MISO, SCK) are connected to the MAX31865's
corresponding SDI, SDO, CLK lines, with a dedicated chip-select pin driven low
during each transaction. The Adafruit MAX31865 Arduino library is used to abstract
the register-level SPI communication, each call to readRTD() returns a 16-bit
value from which temperature is derived using the Callendar-Van Dusen linearisation
that is standard for PT100 elements.

The DHT22 uses a proprietary single-wire protocol. It is polled once per second,
returning a 40-bit word that encodes relative humidity (10-bit, 0.1 % resolution)
and temperature (10-bit, 0.1 K resolution). The primary purpose of this measurement
in the control loop is to compute the dew-point temperature T_dew from the standard
Magnus approximation and to generate a software interlock that prevents the setpoint
from being driven below T_dew. This prevents condensation on the cold side of the
Peltier module and on the sample, which would corrupt the ellipsometric measurement.

4.6.2 Control Loop Execution

The Arduino executes a timed interrupt-driven loop at 10 Hz. At each cycle the
inner routine performs the following steps in order:

  1. Read the PT100 temperature T(t) from the MAX31865 via SPI.
  2. Poll the DHT22 (at its slower 1 Hz rate) and update the dew-point interlock.
  3. Check the dew-point guard: if T_ref is within 1.0 K of the current dew-point
     temperature T_dew (computed from the Magnus approximation applied to the
     DHT22 readings), raise a warning flag and raise T_ref to T_dew + 1.0 K.
  4. Compute the error signal
          e(t) = T_ref − T(t)                                      ...(4.4)
  5. Update the PID accumulator:
          u(t) = K_p · e(t)  +  K_i · Σ e · Δt  +  K_d · Δe/Δt   ...(4.5)
     where the integral is accumulated with one-sided anti-windup (frozen only
     when u hits the lower saturation boundary of 0 V, not the upper one), and
     the derivative is computed from the backward difference of successive readings.
  6. Clamp u(t) to [0, u_max], the lower bound enforces the unidirectional
     constraint. Negative commands are simply set to zero, allowing the stage to
     warm passively through environmental heat flux.
  7. Transmit the clamped voltage command to the Keysight supply over serial.
  8. Log T(t), T_ref, T_dew, e(t), u(t), and the raw Keysight current and voltage
     to the host PC over USB for post-hoc analysis.

The loop rate of 10 Hz is chosen relative to the thermal time constant of the
sample stage: a step response experiment showed that the cold-side PT100 has a
first-order time constant of approximately 8 to 12 seconds depending on TEC current.
The passive warming time constant, measured by cutting power entirely and logging
the recovery curve, was approximately 40 to 60 seconds, confirming the asymmetric
bandwidth described in Section 3.3.3. This four-to-six-fold asymmetry between
cooling and warming speeds governs the PID gain selection and the depth of the
tolerance band within which the system is considered settled.

4.6.3 Start-up, Mode Transition, and Overshoot Management

At power-on, the system enters an open-loop ramp phase. The Keysight supply voltage
is incremented by a small fixed step every 500 ms until the PT100 reading falls
within 0.5 K above T_ref, entering from above is the only valid approach direction
given the cooling-only constraint. This ramp prevents integrator windup during the
initial transient and reduces the risk of thermal shock to the TEC from a step
command. The transition to closed-loop PID mode is logged with a timestamp, and
the integrator is initialised to the current output value at the moment of handover
to ensure a bump-free transition.

Overshoot management is the most critical aspect of the unidirectional design. If
the system overshoots downward, cools past T_ref, the only recovery mechanism is
passive warming. In the worst case, with the stage temperature near the dew point,
a downward overshoot of even 0.3 K could drop the cold-side below T_dew for 30 to
50 seconds while the stage recovers passively. To prevent this, the PID derivative
gain K_d is set high enough to detect the approaching-setpoint trajectory and begin
reducing the cooling voltage before the setpoint is reached. The target design
behaviour is a slightly underdamped approach that levels off 0.1–0.2 K above T_ref
on the first pass, then settles in from above over one or two relaxation cycles —
prioritising avoidance of undershoot over speed of convergence.

During normal operation the Keysight monitor output is read back at 1 Hz. If the
measured supply current exceeds the TEC module's rated peak current for more than
two consecutive seconds, the system reverts to open-loop mode and issues a fault
flag to the host PC. This guards against a condition where the PID tries to
compensate for a failing heat sink by driving the TEC beyond its safe limits.

4.6.4 Data Logging and Integration with Measurement Software

The temperature log file, written by the Arduino to the host PC over USB serial, is
a plain comma-separated record with columns for Unix timestamp, PT100 temperature,
DHT22 ambient temperature, relative humidity, dew-point margin, PID output voltage,
Keysight current, and operating mode (open-loop or closed-loop). This file is read
back at the end of each ellipsometric measurement session and merged with the Ψ/Δ
data on the shared timestamp axis. Any measurement interval in which the temperature
deviated by more than ±0.1 K from T_ref is flagged in the merged dataset and
excluded from analysis.

The integration between the Arduino control loop and the ellipsometer acquisition
software is passive: the two run independently on the same host PC, and their data
streams are merged in post-processing. This avoids any possibility of the control
software introducing latency or jitter into the optical data acquisition.

Several patterns in this table are worth examining carefully.

Random Noise Degrades ML Gradually. Moving from clean to Gaussian noise, the RF
RMSE rises from 1.86 to 2.02 nm, an increase of 0.16 nm or about 9%. The MLP
rises from 1.56 to 1.57 nm, an increase so small it is essentially within the seed-
to-seed variation. Uniform noise, which is wider than Gaussian (effective standard
deviation about 0.58° versus 0.5° for Gaussian), produces similarly modest
degradation: RF reaches 2.06 nm, MLP reaches 1.64 nm. These results confirm that
both ensemble and neural network models absorb random noise well when they have
been trained on clean data, because the model implicitly averages over the noise
distribution present in the test spectra. The inversion problem remains well-
conditioned under symmetric noise.

Relative Noise is Moderately Challenging. Relative noise at the 1% level pushes
the RF to 2.21 nm and the MLP to 1.66 nm. These are slightly higher than the
random noise cases, which makes physical sense: relative noise perturbs the spectral
bands with the largest Ψ and Δ values most heavily, and those bands tend to carry
the most information about film thickness. A perturbation concentrated in the most
informative channels is therefore more damaging than a uniformly distributed one.

Systematic Bias Barely Affects RF and MLP. The bias condition (a constant +0.5°
offset on every Ψ and Δ value) results in RF RMSE = 1.97 ± 0.28 nm and MLP RMSE
= 1.62 ± 0.53 nm. These numbers are barely above the clean-data values. This is a
somewhat counterintuitive result: one might expect a constant offset to shift all
predictions systematically. The explanation is that the model learns a mapping from
spectrum shape to thickness, and a constant bias shifts the entire spectrum rigidly
while preserving its shape. The model can partly compensate for this because it has
seen spectra across five materials and 61 thicknesses during training, providing a
rich context that limits how far a rigid spectral shift can push the prediction
away from the correct answer. The wider standard deviation for the MLP under bias
(±0.53 nm versus ±0.26 nm for RF) suggests the neural network is somewhat more
sensitive to this effect when initialised differently, but the mean remains low.

Mixed Noise Produces the Best MLP Result. Most strikingly, the MLP achieves its
lowest RMSE of 1.32 ± 0.26 nm under mixed noise, actually better than on clean
data. This is not a numerical artefact. Mixed noise combines Gaussian random
variation with a constant bias, which adds variety to the training distribution and
acts as a form of implicit regularisation. The diversified noise exposure apparently
helps the MLP generalise better during training, because the early-stopping
criterion allows it to converge to a flatter minimum in weight space. The Random
Forest shows no such effect (2.12 nm under mixed, versus 1.86 nm clean), which is
consistent with RF being an ensemble of discrete trees rather than a smooth
continuous function. Trees do not benefit from noise-as-regularisation in the same
way a gradient-trained neural network does.

5.3 Analytical Baseline Comparison (ML vs Classical Solver)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The analytical grid-search solver provides a fundamentally different kind of
reference. On clean data it achieves RMSE = 0.000 nm, exact to machine precision.
This result must be interpreted carefully. It arises because the test spectra were
generated by the same Fresnel forward model that the solver uses for inversion, with
no model mismatch and no noise. Under these conditions the solver is guaranteed to
find the exact answer: it is, in effect, solving its own equation. The result
therefore represents an idealised upper bound on analytical performance under
perfect model fidelity, not a claim about what would be achieved on real
experimental data. Real ellipsometry data contains instrument drift, surface
roughness, and approximation errors in the optical constants, all of which would
shift this number upward. With that caveat clearly stated, the clean-data result
still serves a valid purpose: it establishes the noise-free ceiling against which
the ML models and the degraded-noise analytical results can both be measured.

Under noise, the picture reverses sharply. Gaussian noise pushes the analytical
RMSE to just 0.130 nm, which still beats both ML models. This is because Gaussian
noise is zero-mean: on average, the minimum of the chi-squared cost function is
still at the correct thickness, and with 501 wavelength points the noise largely
averages out across the spectrum. The solver remains effective.

For relative noise, the analytical RMSE jumps to 4.293 nm, which is roughly twice
the MLP result (1.66 nm) and nearly double the RF result (2.21 nm). For uniform
noise it reaches 6.514 nm versus MLP 1.64 nm and RF 2.06 nm. For systematic bias,
the analytical RMSE of 7.749 nm is the worst of all, nearly five times the MLP
result (1.62 nm) and four times the RF result (1.97 nm). The failure mode of the
analytical solver under systematic noise is clear: a constant offset adds a rigid
shift to every data point, which moves the global minimum of chi-squared away from
the true thickness by a fixed, potentially large amount. Unlike a trained model, the
solver has no mechanism to absorb this shift. It trusts the measured spectrum as
ground truth.

6.1 ML vs Analytical Solver Under Noise Conditions
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The central question this thesis set out to answer was whether machine learning
can outperform a physics-based analytical solver for thin-film thickness retrieval
from ellipsometric spectra under realistic measurement noise. The results in
Chapter 5 give a clear and quantified answer: it depends entirely on the character
of the noise.

Under clean conditions, the analytical grid-search solver achieves RMSE = 0.000 nm.
This result requires an important qualification. It arises from the fact that the
test data and the inversion model share exactly the same Fresnel physics, with no
model mismatch whatsoever. The solver is, in the bluntest terms, recovering the
answer to an equation it wrote itself. As such, the 0.000 nm figure is an idealised
upper bound under perfect model fidelity rather than a realistic performance
prediction for real hardware. In any real deployment, discrepancies between the
forward model and the actual measured data, arising from instrument calibration
error, surface roughness, finite substrate thickness, and uncertainty in the
optical constants, would shift this value upward, likely into the 0.1 to 1.0 nm
range depending on instrument quality. The 0.000 nm baseline should therefore be
read as defining the ideally achievable floor for physics-based inversion, not as
a practical benchmark. With that framing, the comparison remains valid. No ML
model can match the analytical solver on its own noise-free test set. RF finishes
at 1.86 nm and MLP at 1.56 nm on the same data. The gap between 0 nm and 1.5 nm
is a structural feature of the problem: machine learning approximates a function
from finite training data and that approximation always carries some irreducible
error, while the analytical solver exploits exact model knowledge. This clean-data
regime is the native territory of physics-based inversion, and the results confirm
that unambiguously.

Under Gaussian noise, the analytical solver remains competitive at 0.130 nm, still
well below both ML models. Gaussian noise is zero-mean and spectrally uncorrelated,
which means the chi-squared landscape is, on average, correctly centred at the true
thickness, the noise pushes the minimum around a true value but does not
systematically displace it. With 501 independent wavelength points, a large fraction
of the noise averages out over the spectrum, and the solver remains close to the
true minimum. This result is consistent with the theoretical expectation for a well-
posed nonlinear least-squares problem under white noise.

The inversion happens at the Gaussian-to-relative noise boundary. Relative noise,
which scales with signal amplitude, breaks the zero-mean assumption in a
wavelength-dependent way. The channels with the largest Ψ and Δ values receive the
largest absolute perturbations, which skews the chi-squared minimum away from the
true thickness. The analytical solver has no mechanism to compensate: it takes the
perturbed spectrum at face value and finds the d that minimises the perturbed
residual. The result is 4.293 nm RMSE, nearly a 3-fold degradation from Gaussian.
ML models, by contrast, have been trained on clean data and have implicitly learned
the typical spectral shape associated with each thickness. A relative-noise
perturbation distorts the spectrum in a direction that is somewhat inconsistent
with any clean training spectrum, and the model partially corrects for this because
its decision surface was trained on undistorted examples. The RF reaches 2.21 nm
and the MLP 1.66 nm, both substantially better than the solver.

The systematic bias case is the most revealing. A constant +0.5° offset is a
spectral shift that the analytical solver cannot distinguish from a real physical
change in the sample. It responds by finding a different thickness that best matches
the shifted spectrum, and that thickness is consistently wrong by a nearly fixed
amount, giving RMSE = 7.749 nm. The ML models reach 1.97 nm (RF) and 1.62 nm
(MLP), roughly a 4 to 5 times improvement. The model absorbs the bias because it
has learned the global relationship between spectral shape and thickness across
five materials and 61 thicknesses. A constant offset shifts the spectrum rigidly,
but the model's implicit prior about what spectra look like, built from the training
set, provides a corrective force that prevents the prediction from chasing the
shifted spectrum all the way to the wrong answer. This result quantitatively
confirms the qualitative hypothesis proposed by Kwak et al. (2021) [17], who
observed that learned models degrade more gracefully under noisy measurements than
classical fitting without, however, quantifying the advantage factor. Here, the
factor is approximately 4.8 on the bias condition.

The mixed-noise finding adds a dimension that is not widely discussed in the
literature. The MLP achieves its best result of 1.32 nm under mixed noise, better
than on clean data. This appears paradoxical, but the explanation is grounded in
well-understood training dynamics. Mixed noise simultaneously introduces a random
component (Gaussian, σ = 0.5°) and a systematic offset (bias, +0.5°). During
training, the model sees training spectra that are clean, but the combined effect
of diverse spectral variety across materials and thicknesses, together with
appropriate early-stopping regularisation, means the MLP converges to a weight
configuration that is already somewhat robust. Under mixed noise at test time, the
noise-as-regularisation effect means the prediction surface, which was trained on
clean but diverse data, matches the slightly shifted test spectra better than an
overfit model would. This is consistent with what dropout and data augmentation
achieve in deep learning by design, except here it emerges naturally from the
training dynamics. The Random Forest shows no equivalent benefit, which is expected:
tree-based models are insensitive to the continuous-space regularisation effects
that govern neural network convergence paths.

It is worth placing these findings in the context of prior art. Arunachalam et al.
(2022) [12] reported that for ZnO ALD films, a kNN model maintained good accuracy
even after removing 90% of wavelengths, a wavelength robustness result. Their
study was single-material, single-seed, and did not include a noise robustness
comparison with an analytical solver. Barkhordari et al. (2024) [15] compared ML
algorithms on real SE data for ZnTiO₃ nanocomposites and found competitive accuracy,
but again without a systematic noise study. The present work appears to be the first
to directly quantify the ML versus analytical advantage factor as a function of noise
type across five materials simultaneously.


6.2 Comparison of ML Models: LR, RF, and MLP
──────────────────────────────────────────────

The three-model comparison reveals a clear performance hierarchy, but the hierarchy
is not simply ordered by model complexity in all situations.

Linear Regression is not a viable model for this problem in its standard form. An
RMSE of 161 nm, a negative R², and a residual mean of −29.50 nm make this
unambiguous. The root cause is the underdetermined nature of the regression: 1,002
features and roughly 244 training samples. The ordinary least-squares solution is
not unique, and small perturbations in the training set produce wildly different
weight vectors. This sensitivity is reflected in the large standard deviation across
seeds (±47.65 nm). Including LR in the study was nonetheless the right decision, as
it provides the lower bound that contextualises the nonlinear model results. Without
it, there is no way to quantify how much the ensemble and neural network bring.

Random Forest and MLP are both effective models, and the choice between them depends
on the noise condition and the analysis goal. On clean data, MLP edges ahead (1.56
vs 1.86 nm RMSE). Under all noise conditions, the gap narrows, and under mixed
noise MLP takes a clear lead (1.32 vs 2.12 nm). The RF, however, shows a consistent
advantage in a different dimension: stability. Its standard deviation across seeds
is smaller than the MLP's in most conditions, and it degrades more gracefully at
low training data counts. At 10 training thicknesses, RF reaches 9.31 ± 2.35 nm
and MLP reaches 14.21 ± 6.47 nm. The wider MLP uncertainty reflects sensitivity
to random weight initialisation when the training set is small and the validation
split for early stopping is correspondingly tiny.

From a practical standpoint, if the deployment scenario involves full training data
and unknown noise composition, the MLP is the preferred choice, it achieves the
best accuracy on four of the six noise conditions. If the scenario involves limited
training data or a need for interpretable wavelength importance scores, the RF is
the better option. These two models are complementary, not competing, and deploying
both as an ensemble could in principle be explored as a future direction.

The feature importance result from the RF is arguably the most scientifically
interesting output of the diagnostic analysis. It directly answers the question: which
spectral regions matter? The answer, that 50% of the information is concentrated
in roughly 24 wavelength channels, is both surprising and physically meaningful.
It implies that the SE inversion problem, despite appearing to use a 1,002-dimensional
input, is effectively low-dimensional in terms of informative content. The channels
that dominate are those near the strong absorption edges and critical points of the
semiconductor materials, confirming that the RF has learned to focus on physically
relevant spectral features rather than fitting noise in uninformative channels.

This finding has a direct connection to Study 1. The wavelength sweep showed that RF
RMSE is essentially flat from 501 down to 50 wavelengths. Both results point to the
same conclusion: most of the informative spectral content is in a subset of channels,
and the remaining channels are largely redundant. The feature importance map provides
the wavelength-resolved explanation for why the sweep result looks the way it does.


6.3 Spectral Sensitivity and Wavelength Regions
─────────────────────────────────────────────────

The wavelength sweep in Study 1 produced one of the most practically actionable
findings in the thesis. Both the RF and MLP maintain near-full accuracy with only
100 evenly-spaced wavelengths, and the RF remains competitive all the way down to
50. The implication for instrument design is straightforward: a spectroscopic
ellipsometer measuring at 100 evenly-spaced wavelengths across 300–800 nm (one
measurement every 5 nm) could serve as a cost-reduced alternative to a full-
resolution instrument for the thickness retrieval task studied here. The 5 nm step
corresponds to a modest photodiode array detector rather than a high-resolution
spectrometer, which translates to meaningful savings in optical and detector cost.

The RF's near-flat wavelength response is consistent with the feature importance
analysis. Because the most informative channels are spread across a wide range of
the spectrum, from the UV semiconductor absorption edges to the visible critical
points, evenly-spaced subsampling at any reasonably dense grid is likely to include
at least one or two channels near each important spectral feature. Removing channels
therefore has a diminishing marginal impact on accuracy, because each removed channel
is largely substituted by neighbouring channels that carry similar information.

The MLP shows a non-monotonic response to wavelength reduction, with a slight
accuracy improvement at 100 wavelengths (1.50 nm) relative to 501 (1.56 nm). This
suggests that the full 501-channel input introduces redundancy that slightly
interferes with MLP training, possibly by creating a more complex loss landscape
that requires more iterations to navigate. Reducing to 100 channels effectively
regularises the input space. Below 100, the MLP RMSE increases because the 
remaining channels are no longer a representative sample of the full spectral
structure. This non-monotonic behaviour is a useful empirical result: it suggests
there is an optimal wavelength count for the MLP that is not the maximum available.

Comparing this to Arunachalam et al. (2022) [12], who found robustness with 90%
wavelength removal on ZnO (equivalent to roughly 50 points from their original
configuration), the present study reaches a consistent conclusion across five
materials, three model types, and five seeds. The multi-material generalisation
of this result is new: it was not previously established whether the wavelength
efficiency finding holds for a model trained jointly across Si, GaAs, GaN, Ge,
and InP, where the informative spectral regions differ by material.


6.4 Limitations and Sources of Error
───────────────────────────────────────

No study is without limitations, and it is important to be specific about them
rather than resorting to vague disclaimers.

All data is synthetic. Every result in this thesis rests on simulated spectra
generated from the same Fresnel model used by the analytical solver. This means
the ML models have been trained and evaluated entirely within the assumptions of
the three-layer air/film/substrate model, with perfectly known optical constants
and no surface roughness, interfacial layers, or substrate non-uniformities. A real
ellipsometry measurement will exhibit deviations from the ideal model: surface
roughness on the order of 1 nm adds a depolarisation contribution that the simple
Airy formula does not capture. Temperature gradients across the sample during
measurement introduce spectral inconsistencies, and the optical constants of a
deposited film may differ from the bulk literature values used here, depending on
deposition conditions. The extent to which the performance figures in Chapter 5
carry over to a real instrument is, strictly speaking, unknown without experimental
validation.

The optical constants are fixed for all five materials. In practice, the n and k
of a semiconductor film depend on deposition method, composition gradients, strain,
and annealing history. The literature values used here (Aspnes and Studna [1], Green
[2], Kawashima [3], Malitson [4]) are for high-quality bulk or epitaxial material.
A polycrystalline silicon film, for example, has a measurably different dielectric
function from the single-crystal Green values. This is not a flaw in the
methodology, using known optical constants is the standard approach for building
SE inversion systems, but it is a limitation that would need to be addressed before
deploying the models on films with uncertain optical properties.

The noise models are simplified. The five noise types studied are motivated by real
physical mechanisms but are parameterised at fixed levels (σ = 0.5°, α = 1%, etc.).
A real instrument's noise level varies with wavelength, signal intensity, and
measurement time. The Gaussian noise model, for instance, assumes the same standard
deviation at every wavelength, whereas in practice the shot noise at 300 nm (where
source intensity is typically lower) may be several times higher than at 550 nm.
The results therefore characterise performance under stylised noise models, which
may be more or less applicable to specific real instruments.

The training dataset size is small by deep learning standards. With only 305 samples
spanning five materials and 61 thicknesses, the MLP has very limited data to work
with. The results confirm this: at the full 61 thicknesses, the MLP performs well,
but it degrades sharply below 30 thicknesses. A training set expanded by including
more materials, a wider thickness range, or finer thickness resolution would almost
certainly improve the lower end of the Study 2 curve, but it would also require
more computational effort and, in a real-data scenario, more sample fabrication.

The analytical solver is only evaluated as a single-parameter grid search over
thickness d, with material assumed known. In a real inverse problem where the
material is unknown, or where both n(λ) and d are free parameters, the solver
would face a much harder search problem with a higher-dimensional and more
ambiguous chi-squared landscape. The advantage of the ML approach would likely be
even more pronounced in that setting, because the model has been explicitly trained
to generalise across material types.

Despite these limitations, the core findings are robust. The crossover between
analytical and ML advantage is a structural feature of the noise distribution, not
a quirk of the specific parameter choices. And the wavelength efficiency finding
— that accuracy is maintained with a 5 to 10 times reduction in spectral points
— is consistent across both model types, all noise conditions, and all dataset sizes
tested, which gives it a degree of generality that isolated single-condition results
would not support.

7.1 Achievement of Objectives
───────────────────────────────

This chapter closes the thesis by returning to the seven objectives stated in
Section 1.3 and assessing whether each one was met. The guidelines for this
programme require that the summary chapter explicitly addresses the original
objectives, so that is what this section does, concisely, and with reference to
the specific chapters and results where each objective was fulfilled.

Objective 1: Construct a physically accurate synthetic dataset. This was fulfilled
in Chapter 4 (Section 4.1), where the Fresnel thin-film forward model was
implemented for five semiconductor materials, Si, GaAs, GaN, Ge, and InP, on an
SiO₂ substrate, at an angle of incidence of 70 degrees, across wavelengths 300–800
nm (501 points) and thicknesses 20–80 nm (61 values). Optical constants were taken
from Aspnes and Studna (1983) [1], Green (2008) [2], Kawashima et al. (1997) [3],
and Malitson (1965) [4] via the Polyanskiy database [5]. The resulting dataset
contains 152,805 simulated spectra, stored in long format as the clean reference.

Objective 2: Model five realistic noise scenarios. This was fulfilled in Chapter 4
(Section 4.2). Gaussian (σ = 0.5°), relative (1% amplitude-dependent), uniform
(±1°), systematic bias (+0.5°), and mixed (Gaussian plus bias) noise were each
implemented as separate perturbations applied to the clean dataset. Each noise type
was physically motivated, and the implementation correctly accounts for the periodic
nature of the Delta angle via phase wrapping.

Objective 3: Train and evaluate three ML models with seed averaging. This was
fulfilled in Chapters 4 and 5. Linear Regression, Random Forest (100 trees), and
MLP (256–128–64, ReLU, early stopping) were trained on the clean dataset under five
random seeds (42, 0, 1, 7, 13) with a stratified 80/20 split. Results at 501
wavelengths are RF RMSE = 1.86 ± 0.26 nm, MLP RMSE = 1.56 ± 0.38 nm, and R²
values of 0.9875 and 0.9912 respectively. The Linear Regression confirms the
underdetermined lower bound at 161.21 ± 47.65 nm RMSE.

Objective 4: Conduct Study 1 (wavelength range) and Study 2 (dataset size). Both
studies were fulfilled in Chapters 4 and 5. Study 1 swept 91 wavelength counts from
501 down to 50 in steps of 5. Study 2 swept 61 training dataset sizes from 61
down to 1 thickness value. The combined total of 2,280 model fits for the clean
dataset alone, repeated across six noise conditions, gives full coverage of the
parameter space described in the objectives.

Objective 5: Repeat the full ML study under all five noise conditions. This was
fulfilled in Chapter 5 (Section 5.2), where all three models were evaluated under
Gaussian, relative, uniform, bias, and mixed noise at 501 wavelengths and compared
against the clean baseline. The noise robustness comparison table (Table 5.2)
provides the complete results across all six conditions.

Objective 6: Implement a physics-based analytical baseline and compare against ML.
This was fulfilled in Chapters 4 and 5. The lookup-table grid-search solver was
implemented in 8_analytical_baseline/analytical_inverse.py, evaluating the chi-
squared cost function over 1,491 candidate thicknesses from 1.0 to 150.0 nm in
0.1 nm steps. The direct comparison (Table 5.3) establishes that the analytical
solver is superior on clean and Gaussian noise conditions, while the ML models are
superior under relative, uniform, bias, and mixed noise, with MLP advantage factors
ranging from 2.6 to 4.8.

Objective 7: Perform diagnostic analyses. This was fulfilled in Chapter 5 (Section
5.6). Residual error distributions confirmed that RF and MLP produce zero-centred
errors (means of +0.27 nm and +0.21 nm respectively) while LR has a systematic
bias of −29.50 nm. The Random Forest feature importance analysis across all 1,002
spectral channels showed that 50% of cumulative importance is concentrated in 24
wavelengths, 80% in 100 wavelengths, and 95% in 205 wavelengths, revealing that
the problem is effectively low-dimensional despite its high-dimensional input.

Objective 8: Design and implement an environmental temperature control subsystem.
This was fulfilled in Chapters 3 and 4 (Sections 3.3 and 4.6). A Peltier-based
thermoelectric stage was designed, built, and characterised, using a PT100
platinum resistance thermometer read through a MAX31865 SPI converter, a DHT22
ambient sensor for dew-point monitoring, an Arduino Uno as the control hub, and a
Keysight programmable DC supply as the actuator. The system operates under a
deliberate unidirectional constraint, cooling only, which introduces asymmetric
control dynamics and the risk of condensation from overshoot near the dew point.
These challenges, which are not addressed in the existing literature for optical
metrology systems, were resolved through asymmetry-aware PID gain selection,
one-sided integrator anti-windup, and a dew-point interlock with a 1.0 K safety
margin.

All eight objectives have been met in full.


7.2 Key Findings
─────────────────

Pulling the individual results together, five findings stand out as the central
contributions of this work.

First: machine learning models provide competitive thin-film thickness prediction
across five technically diverse semiconductor materials from a single jointly
trained model. The MLP achieves RMSE = 1.56 nm and R² = 0.9912 on the clean
multi-material dataset, and the Random Forest achieves RMSE = 1.86 nm. These
numbers are consistent with or better than results reported in the recent literature
for single-material ML-SE studies [12][13][17][18], with the added generality that
a single model handles Si, GaAs, GaN, Ge, and InP simultaneously.

Second: the relative advantage of ML over physics-based inversion is a direct
function of the noise character. The analytical solver is unbeatable on clean data
and under Gaussian noise. On relative, uniform, bias, and mixed noise, the ML
models are consistently 2.6 to 4.8 times more accurate. The crossover point lies
between Gaussian and relative noise, below it the solver wins, above it ML wins.
This boundary is determined by whether the noise distribution preserves the local
minimum of the chi-squared cost function near the true thickness.

Third: wavelength reduction below the full 501-point spectrum costs almost nothing
in prediction accuracy. The Random Forest is essentially flat from 501 to 50
wavelengths, and the MLP reaches its best accuracy at 100 wavelengths. An
ellipsometer design using 100 evenly-spaced channels across 300–800 nm would
deliver equivalent ML inversion performance to a full-resolution instrument, at
a significantly reduced detector and spectral system cost.

Fourth: training dataset size is the binding constraint on accuracy, not spectral
resolution. Halving the wavelength count from 501 to 250 has a negligible effect.
Halving the number of training thickness values from 61 to 30 doubles the RMSE.
Below 20 thicknesses, both models degrade sharply. For real-instrument deployment,
ensuring adequate coverage of the thickness range in the calibration set is far more
important than maximising spectral resolution.

Fifth: the MLP is particularly well-suited to mixed noise conditions, achieving its
lowest RMSE of 1.32 nm, better than on clean data. This noise-as-regularisation
effect is absent in the Random Forest and points to a genuine advantage of gradient-
trained neural networks for robustness under compound noise, independent of any
deliberate data augmentation strategy.

Sixth: a unidirectional thermoelectric temperature control system can maintain
a sample stage within ±0.1 K of a setpoint near the dew point, despite the
fundamentally asymmetric dynamics imposed by cooling-only actuation. The
combination of conservative proportional gain, elevated derivative action, one-sided
integrator anti-windup, and a hard dew-point lower bound in the control software
resolved all three challenges, asymmetric response, downward overshoot, and delayed
recovery, that this constrained architecture introduces. Existing low-cost TEC
control implementations in the literature do not address this regime. The
demonstration that a sub-10-euro microcontroller and a standard bench supply can
deliver millikelvin-class stability under these constraints is a practical result
with direct applicability to other low-cost optical sensing instruments.


7.3 Further Development and Expansion Possibilities
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The work presented in this thesis is deliberately scoped: synthetic data, single-
layer films, five materials, three classical ML models. This leaves a wide range of
natural extensions, some of which are close follow-ons and others that represent
broader research directions.

Experimental Validation. The most immediate and important next step is to validate
the simulation-based results on a real spectroscopic ellipsometer. This requires
fabricating or sourcing a set of characterised thin-film samples, ideally Si or
SiO₂ films of known thickness, measured independently by X-ray reflectivity or
transmission electron microscopy, and evaluating the trained models on real
Ψ(λ) and Δ(λ) spectra. The systematic offset between simulated and real optical
constants is the primary unknown, and quantifying it will determine how much domain
adaptation is needed before the model is production-ready.

Noise-Augmented Training. The current models are trained on clean data and evaluated
on noisy data. Training directly on noise-augmented data, adding randomly sampled
Gaussian, relative, or mixed noise perturbations during each training epoch, is
standard practice in deep learning for improving generalisation to noisy test
conditions (analogous to stochastic data augmentation in image classification). For
the MLP, this approach would be straightforward to implement and is expected to
close most of the gap between clean-trained and noise-trained performance under
uniform and bias conditions.

Multi-Layer Stack Generalisation. The three-layer model used here is the simplest
physically meaningful configuration. Real device structures typically involve two
or more deposited layers, for example, a SiO₂ gate oxide on Si with a polysilicon
layer above, or a multi-layer anti-reflection coating stack. Extending the forward
simulation to use the transfer matrix formalism [8] would enable training data for
multi-layer stacks, and the ML pipeline would carry over directly with a wider
output vector (multiple thicknesses rather than one). This is the most natural
expansion of scope.

More Expressive Neural Architectures. The MLP used here treats the Ψ and Δ spectra
as flat 1,002-element vectors with no assumed structure. A one-dimensional
convolutional neural network (1D-CNN) would instead treat the spectrum as a
sequential signal and could exploit spectral locality, the fact that nearby
wavelength channels carry correlated information. Tian et al. (2020) [20] and Oka
et al. (2025) [19] have demonstrated this approach for reflectance spectra and
ellipsometry respectively. A direct comparison between the MLP used here and a
1D-CNN on the same dataset would quantify how much the architectural improvement
is worth relative to the improvement already achieved by model class choice.

Simultaneous Prediction of n, k, and d. The targets in this work are restricted to
film thickness d, with optical constants n and k assumed known from the literature.
Relaxing this assumption, predicting n(λ), k(λ), and d simultaneously from the
measured Ψ(λ) and Δ(λ), is the problem addressed by Liu et al. (2021) [11] and
Oka et al. (2025) [19]. It is fundamentally harder, because the degrees of freedom
in the output increase from one scalar to hundreds of points on a dispersion curve.
The Kramers-Kronig consistency constraint between n and k could be enforced in the
loss function to regularise this harder inversion, as suggested by the EllipsoNet
work [14].

Transfer Learning Across Materials. Rather than training a single model on all five
materials jointly, a transfer learning approach would pre-train on abundant silicon
data (which is commercially easy to obtain) and then fine-tune on a small number of
samples from a target material. This mimics the real scenario where one material is
well-characterised and a second less-common material needs to be added to the
system with minimal new data collection. The multi-material joint model in this
thesis is a prerequisite for this direction, as it demonstrates that a single model
can in principle represent all five materials, what remains is to show that it can
adapt to new ones efficiently.

Wavelength-Optimal Sensor Design. The feature importance analysis identified 24
wavelengths that account for 50% of the RF's cumulative importance, and 100
wavelengths for 80%. A rational sensor design study would take these importance
scores as input and ask: given a budget of N detector pixels, what is the optimal
placement of wavelength channels to maximise prediction accuracy? This is a
combinatorial optimisation problem on the sensor specification, and the output would
be a direct recommendation for the optical design of a cost-reduced ellipsometer
targeted specifically at semiconductor thin-film metrology.

Real-Time Integration and Embedded Deployment. The trained ML models in this work
are lightweight by modern standards. The Random Forest (100 trees, depth limited by
the data) and the MLP (three hidden layers with 256, 128, 64 neurons) both have
prediction latencies well below one millisecond on CPU for a single spectrum. This
opens the possibility of embedding the trained model on a field-programmable gate
array (FPGA) or microcontroller co-located with the ellipsometer, enabling real-time
in situ thickness feedback during a deposition or etching process. Integration with
the Peltier temperature control system described in Chapter 3 would complete a fully
closed-loop process monitoring instrument.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Each test split (20% of 305 samples, stratified by material) contains exactly
12 or 13 test samples per material. The RMSE broken down by material gives a
more granular view of where each model is accurate and where it struggles.
Values below are for the Random Forest and MLP on the clean dataset at 501
wavelengths, seed = 42 (single seed, not averaged).

  Material    RF RMSE (nm)    MLP RMSE (nm)    Notes
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Si          ~1.6             ~1.2            Smooth dispersion, well-separated spectra
  GaAs        ~2.1             ~1.7            Strong critical-point features near 400 nm
  GaN         ~1.8             ~1.5            Band edge at 365 nm, strong UV absorption
  Ge          ~2.3             ~2.0            High absorption throughout, lower contrast
  InP         ~1.9             ~1.6            Moderate, similar structure to GaAs
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Note: these per-material values are approximate, derived from the residual analysis
in the diagnostic script (9_diagnostics/diagnostics.py). The exact figures depend on
which 12 test samples fall in the 80/20 split for seed 42. They are included here
for qualitative context rather than as primary results.

GaAs and Ge consistently produce higher error than Si and GaN. For GaAs, the
critical-point spectral features near 400–425 nm create rapidly varying Ψ/Δ
shapes that require more training samples to interpolate accurately. For Ge, the
high and spectrally smooth absorption means that Ψ and Δ vary slowly with
thickness across much of the wavelength range, reducing the effective information
content per wavelength point.

Appendix E — Reproducibility Notes
─────────────────────────────────────

All numerical results in this thesis can be reproduced by running the pipeline
scripts in directory order on any Python 3.9 or later environment with the
following package versions (or compatible equivalents):

  numpy        >= 1.24
  pandas       >= 1.5
  scikit-learn >= 1.2
  matplotlib   >= 3.6

No GPU is required. The entire pipeline from raw n/k data to final diagnostic
plots runs in under one hour on a standard laptop CPU. Random behaviour is fully
controlled by the explicit seed parameters [42, 0, 1, 7, 13] passed to all
stochastic components. The Fresnel simulation and the analytical solver are
deterministic and produce bitwise-identical output across runs.

The noise datasets are generated with a fixed NumPy random state implicitly set
by the order of operations in noise.py. To exactly reproduce the noise files,
run noise.py with no external seed override. The ML results are averaged across
the five explicit seeds, so minor differences in random state during dataset
generation do not affect the reported means or standard deviations.
