1. Simultaneous multi-material model (5 semiconductors)
Every published study uses one material — Arunachalam et al. used ZnO, then TiO2. Barkhordari et al. used ZnTiO3 composites. You trained a single model across Si, GaAs, InP, Ge, GaN simultaneously. This is a qualitatively different and harder problem — the model must learn across very different optical responses. No paper found does this.

2. Dense systematic wavelength study (91 cases, 501 → 50, step=5)
Arunachalam et al. did show wavelength robustness, but with random downsampling and only a handful of cases. Your 91-point dense sweep with evenly spaced subsampling is far more rigorous and quantifies exactly where — if anywhere — performance degrades. Your key finding (no degradation down to 50 points for RF/MLP) is more precisely stated than anything in the literature.

3. Dense systematic dataset size study (all 61 cases down to 1)
No equivalent study found. Systematic analysis of how many training thickness values are needed is not addressed in any of the papers found. Your answer (~25+ thicknesses for stable MLP) is original.

4. Multi-seed statistical averaging with ± standard deviation
Papers report single-run results without addressing the sensitivity to train/test splits or model seeds. Your 5-seed averaged study with mean ± std error bands is statistically more rigorous than what is typically published in the field. The finding that Linear Regression is not just bad but highly unstable across seeds is a clean methodological result.

5. A physics-correct from-scratch Fresnel pipeline
You implemented the full forward model yourself (Snell + Fresnel + Airy thin-film formula), including complex refractive index handling, for 5 literature-sourced materials. Most papers use commercial ellipsometry software or pre-existing simulations. Yours is fully reproducible and transparent.

6. Controlled five-type noise taxonomy (partially done)
The systematic classification of 5 noise types (Gaussian, relative, uniform, bias, mixed) with phase wrapping applied to Δ is more principled than what papers do. Kwak et al.
